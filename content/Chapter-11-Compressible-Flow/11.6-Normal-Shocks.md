# Section 11.6: Normal Shocks

> **Core theme:** A normal shock is an abrupt, near-discontinuity in a supersonic flow across which pressure, density, temperature, and entropy rise sharply while velocity drops from supersonic to subsonic. The shock is a **non-isentropic** compression — it converts kinetic energy into thermal energy (internal energy) with a total pressure loss. This is one of the most important and distinctive phenomena in compressible flow.

$$ \text{← upstream (supersonic)} \quad \boxed{\text{SHOCK}} \quad \text{downstream (subsonic) →} $$

***

## 1. What is a Shock Wave?

A shock wave is a **thin, discontinuous surface** that only exists in **supersonic flows** (Ma > 1).

- **Definition:** When a supersonic flow encounters a compression (hitting an obstacle, high back pressure, etc.), pressure disturbances cannot travel upstream because the flow is faster than the speed of sound. These disturbances **pile up** into a sharp jump — a shock wave.
- **Thickness:** Only a few molecular mean free paths (~10⁻⁷ m for air at standard conditions). For engineering purposes, we treat it as a **zero-thickness discontinuity**.
- **Normal vs Oblique:** A **normal shock** is perpendicular to the flow direction. An **oblique shock** is angled. Here we focus on normal shocks.

### 1.1 How Shocks Form — A Deeper Look

The flow "finds out" about downstream obstacles abruptly because sound waves (which carry the information) can't outrun the flow. The supersonic upstream flow slams into the compressed region, creating a stationary discontinuity.

In the $h$-$s$ (Mollier) diagram, shock transitions jump from the supersonic Fanno/Rayleigh branch to the subsonic Fanno/Rayleigh branch — always toward higher entropy.

***

## 2. Control Volume & Governing Equations

### 2.1 Control Volume Setup

We place a control volume that encloses the shock:

- **Upstream (1 or x):** Uniform supersonic flow — $\rho_1$, $p_1$, $T_1$, $V_1$, $\text{Ma}_1$
- **Downstream (2 or y):** Uniform subsonic flow — $\rho_2$, $p_2$, $T_2$, $V_2$, $\text{Ma}_2$

> **Notation note:** Textbook often uses 1→2 or x→y. We use 1→2 here, consistent with the flow direction.

### 2.2 Conservation Laws

Across the shock, the flow is steady and adiabatic. The shock is so thin that wall friction and heat transfer are negligible:

| Law | Equation | Eq. |
|-----|----------|-----|
| **Mass (continuity)** | $\rho_1 V_1 = \rho_2 V_2 = \dot{m}/A$ | (11.60) |
| **Momentum (Euler integrated)** | $p_1 + \rho_1 V_1^2 = p_2 + \rho_2 V_2^2$ | (11.61) |
| **Energy (adiabatic, no work)** | $h_1 + V_1^2/2 = h_2 + V_2^2/2 = h_0$ | (11.62) |
| **Ideal gas (constant $c_p$)** | $c_p T_1 + V_1^2/2 = c_p T_2 + V_2^2/2$ | (11.63) |
| **Ideal gas law** | $p_1 = \rho_1 R T_1$, $p_2 = \rho_2 R T_2$ | (11.64) |

> ⚠️ **Key insight:** The shock is **adiabatic** (no time for heat transfer), but **NOT isentropic** (viscous dissipation inside the shock generates entropy). The **total temperature is constant** ($T_{01} = T_{02}$), but **total pressure drops** ($p_{02} < p_{01}$).

---

## 3. Detailed Derivations (From First Principles)

We now derive the three key relations step by step. All results depend **only** on upstream Mach number $\text{Ma}_1$ and specific heat ratio $k$.

---

### 3.1 Derivation: Pressure Ratio $\displaystyle \frac{p_2}{p_1}$

**Step 1 — Express momentum equation in terms of $\rho V^2$:**

From momentum (11.61): $p_2 - p_1 = \rho_1 V_1^2 - \rho_2 V_2^2$

Using continuity ($\rho_2 V_2 = \rho_1 V_1$):

$$p_2 - p_1 = \rho_1 V_1 (V_1 - V_2) = \rho_1 V_1^2\left(1 - \frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)$$

**Step 2 — Express $\rho_1 V_1^2$ in Mach form:**

Recall $\text{Ma}_1 = V_1/c_1$ and $c_1 = \sqrt{k R T_1}$:

$$\rho_1 V_1^2 = \rho_1 \cdot \text{Ma}_1^2 \cdot k R T_1 = \frac{p_1}{R T_1} \cdot \text{Ma}_1^2 \cdot k R T_1 = k p_1 \text{Ma}_1^2$$

**Step 3 — The Prandtl relation (key intermediate):**

Combining continuity, momentum, and energy yields:

$$V_1 V_2 = c^{*2} \qquad \text{(Prandtl relation)}$$

where $c^*$ is the **critical speed of sound** (speed at Ma = 1 for given stagnation conditions):

$$c^{*2} = \frac{2kRT_0}{k+1} = c_1^2 \cdot \frac{2 + (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2}{k+1}$$

Therefore:

$$\frac{V_2}{V_1} = \frac{c^{*2}}{V_1^2} = \frac{2 + (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2}{(k+1)\text{Ma}_1^2}$$

From continuity, this is also the inverse density ratio:

$$\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_2} = \frac{V_2}{V_1} = \frac{2 + (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2}{(k+1)\text{Ma}_1^2} \qquad \text{(11.69)}$$

**Step 4 — Substitute:**

$$\frac{p_2}{p_1} - 1 = k \text{Ma}_1^2 \left[1 - \frac{2 + (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2}{(k+1)\text{Ma}_1^2}\right]$$

Simplify the bracket:

$$1 - \frac{2 + (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2}{(k+1)\text{Ma}_1^2}
= \frac{(k+1)\text{Ma}_1^2 - 2 - (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2}{(k+1)\text{Ma}_1^2}
= \frac{2(\text{Ma}_1^2 - 1)}{(k+1)\text{Ma}_1^2}$$

**Step 5 — Final result:**

$$\boxed{\frac{p_2}{p_1} = 1 + \frac{2k}{k+1}(\text{Ma}_1^2 - 1)} \qquad \text{(11.67)}$$

Or equivalently:

$$\boxed{\frac{p_2}{p_1} = \frac{2k}{k+1}\text{Ma}_1^2 - \frac{k-1}{k+1}}$$

---

### 3.2 Derivation: Temperature Ratio $\displaystyle \frac{T_2}{T_1}$

Using the ideal gas law:

$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \frac{p_2}{p_1} \cdot \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_2}$$

Substitute the pressure ratio (11.67) and density ratio (11.69):

$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \frac{[2k\text{Ma}_1^2 - (k-1)] \cdot [2 + (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2]}{(k+1)^2 \text{Ma}_1^2}$$

Rewrite the numerator factors:
- $2 + (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2 = 2\left(1 + \frac{k-1}{2}\text{Ma}_1^2\right)$
- $2k\text{Ma}_1^2 - (k-1) = (k-1)\left(\frac{2k}{k-1}\text{Ma}_1^2 - 1\right)$

Substituting and rearranging:

$$\boxed{\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(1 + \frac{k-1}{2}\text{Ma}_1^2\right) \left(\frac{2k}{k-1}\text{Ma}_1^2 - 1\right) \left[\frac{2(k-1)}{(k+1)^2 \text{Ma}_1^2}\right]} \qquad \text{(11.70)}$$

A cleaner equivalent form is:

$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \frac{[2k\text{Ma}_1^2 - (k-1)][2 + (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2]}{(k+1)^2 \text{Ma}_1^2}$$

---

### 3.3 Downstream Mach Number $\text{Ma}_2$

From the definition $\text{Ma}_2 = V_2/\sqrt{kRT_2}$ and $\text{Ma}_1 = V_1/\sqrt{kRT_1}$:

$$\text{Ma}_2^2 = \frac{V_2^2}{V_1^2} \cdot \frac{T_1}{T_2} \cdot \text{Ma}_1^2$$

After substituting $V_2/V_1$ and $T_1/T_2$:

$$\boxed{\text{Ma}_2^2 = \frac{(k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2 + 2}{2k\text{Ma}_1^2 - (k-1)}} \qquad \text{(11.68)}$$

| $\text{Ma}_1$ | $\text{Ma}_2$ (air, $k=1.4$) |
|:-------------:|:---------------------------:|
| 1.0 | 1.000 (infinitely weak shock) |
| 1.5 | 0.701 |
| 2.0 | 0.577 |
| 3.0 | 0.475 |
| 5.0 | 0.415 |
| $\infty$ | $\sqrt{(k-1)/(2k)} = 0.378$ |

> 📌 **Downstream is ALWAYS subsonic** ($\text{Ma}_2 < 1$) for any $\text{Ma}_1 > 1$.

---

### 3.4 Stagnation Pressure Ratio (Total Pressure Loss)

This is the most important engineering parameter — it measures the **irreversibility** of the shock.

$$\boxed{\frac{p_{02}}{p_{01}} = \left[\frac{(k+1)\text{Ma}_1^2}{2 + (k-1)\text{Ma}_1^2}\right]^{\frac{k}{k-1}} \left[\frac{k+1}{2k\text{Ma}_1^2 - (k-1)}\right]^{\frac{1}{k-1}}} \qquad \text{(11.73)}$$

| $\text{Ma}_1$ | $p_{02}/p_{01}$ (air) |
|:-------------:|:---------------------:|
| 1.0 | 1.000 |
| 1.5 | 0.930 |
| 2.0 | 0.721 |
| 2.5 | 0.499 |
| 3.0 | 0.328 |
| 5.0 | 0.062 |

> 📌 **$p_{02} < p_{01}$ always** — total pressure is destroyed across a shock. As $\text{Ma}_1 \to \infty$, $p_{02}/p_{01} \to 0$.

---

## 4. Physical Meaning — What Happens Across a Normal Shock

| Property | Change | Physical Reason |
|----------|--------|-----------------|
| **Pressure $p$** | Sharply ↑ | Adiabatic compression |
| **Density $\rho$** | Sharply ↑ | Molecule spacing reduced; **upper limit** of $(k+1)/(k-1) = 6$ for air |
| **Temperature $T$** | Sharply ↑ | Kinetic energy → thermal energy (internal energy rises) |
| **Velocity $V$** | Sharply ↓ | Momentum transferred to pressure rise |
| **Mach number** | Drops to < 1 | Defining property of normal shocks |
| **Total temp $T_0$** | **Constant** | Adiabatic process — energy conserved |
| **Total pressure $p_0$** | **Decreases** | Entropy increase → available energy lost |
| **Entropy $s$** | **Increases** | $s_2 > s_1$ — satisfies Second Law |

### Why Total Temperature is Constant but Total Pressure Drops

$T_0$ const: energy equation $c_p T + V^2/2 =$ constant → no heat transfer, no work.
$p_0$ drops: entropy generation inside the shock dissipates mechanical energy into internal energy — this lost work potential is measured by the total pressure loss.

---

## 5. Numerical Example (Air, $k=1.4$, $\text{Ma}_1 = 2$)

Let's compute everything:

**1. Downstream Mach number:**
$$\text{Ma}_2^2 = \frac{(0.4)(4) + 2}{(2.8)(4) - 0.4} = \frac{3.6}{10.8} = \frac{1}{3}$$
$$\text{Ma}_2 = 0.577 \quad \text{(subsonic ✓)}$$

**2. Pressure ratio:**
$$\frac{p_2}{p_1} = 1 + \frac{2(1.4)}{2.4}(4 - 1) = 1 + \frac{2.8}{2.4} \cdot 3 = 4.5$$
Pressure increases **4.5×**.

**3. Temperature ratio:**
$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \frac{[2.8(4)-0.4][2+0.4(4)]}{(2.4)^2(4)} = \frac{[10.8][3.6]}{23.04} = \frac{38.88}{23.04} = 1.688$$
Temperature increases **68.8%**.

**4. Density ratio:**
$$\frac{\rho_2}{\rho_1} = \frac{(2.4)(4)}{2 + (0.4)(4)} = \frac{9.6}{3.6} = 2.667$$

**5. Total pressure loss:**
From tables or Eq. (11.73): $p_{02}/p_{01} = 0.721$ — a **28% loss** of available stagnation pressure.

---

## 6. Entropy Change & Second Law

Using the ideal gas entropy equation (11.20):

$$s_2 - s_1 = c_p \ln\frac{T_2}{T_1} - R\ln\frac{p_2}{p_1}$$

For any $\text{Ma}_1 > 1$, we find $s_2 > s_1$ — the shock generates entropy, consistent with the Second Law.

> ⚠️ **Why can't a "rarefaction shock" (subsonic → supersonic) exist?** That would require $s_2 < s_1$, violating the Second Law. Smooth **Prandtl-Meyer expansion fans** handle supersonic acceleration instead.

---

## 7. Shock Strength

$$\text{Shock strength} = \frac{p_2 - p_1}{p_1} = \frac{2k}{k+1}(\text{Ma}_1^2 - 1)$$

| Regime | Characteristics |
|--------|----------------|
| $\text{Ma}_1 \approx 1$ | Weak shock - acoustic waves (nearly isentropic) |
| $\text{Ma}_1 = 2$ | Moderate $4.5\times$ pressure jump |
| $\text{Ma}_1 = 5$ | Strong shock — $29\times$ pressure jump, $p_{02}/p_{01} = 0.062$ |

---

## 8. Shock Propagation (Moving Shocks)

A **moving shock** (blast wave, shock tube) can be analyzed by Galilean transformation:
- Transform to a frame where the shock is stationary
- Apply all the stationary-shock relations
- Transform back

The relative Mach number in the shock-fixed frame determines the shock strength.

## Key Takeaways

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| $\text{Ma}_2 < 1$ always | Normal shocks are supersonic→subsonic transitions only |
| $p_{02} < p_{01}$ | Measures irreversibility; shocks destroy available energy |
| All ratios depend only on $\text{Ma}_1$ and $k$ | Simple closed-form relations from conservation laws |
| $p_{02}/p_{01} \to 0$ as $\text{Ma}_1 \to \infty$ | Very strong shocks are extremely lossy |
| Adiabatic but not isentropic | Energy conserved, entropy increased |
| $\rho_2/\rho_1 \le (k+1)/(k-1)$ | Density jump has a maximum limit (6 for air) |
