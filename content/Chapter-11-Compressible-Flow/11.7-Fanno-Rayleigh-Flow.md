# Section 11.7: Duct Flow with Friction & Heat Transfer

> **Core theme:** Real compressible flows are never perfectly isentropic. Two important non-isentropic effects occur in constant-area ducts: **(1) Fanno flow** — friction acts on the fluid; **(2) Rayleigh flow** — heat is added or removed. Both can cause choking, and both produce characteristically different behavior depending on whether the flow is subsonic or supersonic.

***

## Part A: Fanno Flow (Adiabatic, Constant Area, Friction)

### A1. Physical Picture

Gas flows through a constant-area duct (pipe) with wall friction. The flow is adiabatic (no heat transfer through walls). Friction converts mechanical energy into thermal energy (internal energy rise).

### A2. Governing Equations

| Law | Equation |
|-----|----------|
| **Continuity** | $\rho V = G = \text{const}$ (mass velocity) |
| **Momentum** | $-dp - \frac{4\tau_w}{D}dx = \rho V dV$ (includes wall shear $\tau_w$) |
| **Energy (adiabatic)** | $c_p T + V^2/2 = c_p T_0 = \text{const}$ |
| **Ideal gas** | $p = \rho RT$ |
| **Friction factor** | $\tau_w = \frac{1}{2}\rho V^2 f$, where $f$ is the Darcy friction factor |

### A3. Fanno Line on $h$-$s$ Diagram

The entire family of states that satisfy continuity and energy for a given mass flow and stagnation enthalpy lies on a curve called the **Fanno line**:

- **Subsonic branch:** entropy increases as velocity increases
- **Supersonic branch:** entropy increases as velocity decreases
- **Maximum entropy point:** occurs at Ma = 1 (sonic) — the **sonic point** is the limit of physical flow

> 📌 **The entropy maximum at Ma = 1 means:** Fanno flow can only approach Ma = 1 through friction. It cannot cross Ma = 1 in a constant-area duct with friction alone.

### A4. Fanno Flow Relations

The key working equation gives the relationship between duct length, friction, and Mach number:

$$\boxed{\frac{4fL_{\text{max}}}{D} = \frac{1 - \text{Ma}^2}{k\,\text{Ma}^2} + \frac{k+1}{2k} \ln\left[\frac{(k+1)\,\text{Ma}^2}{2\left(1 + \frac{k-1}{2}\text{Ma}^2\right)}\right]} \qquad \text{(11.84)}$$

Where:
- $L_{\text{max}}$ = the duct length required for friction to accelerate/decelerate the flow from its current Ma to Ma = 1
- $f$ = Darcy friction factor (assumed constant)
- $D$ = duct diameter

### A5. Fanno Behavior Summary

| Starting Condition | Effect of Friction | Limitation |
|-------------------|-------------------|------------|
| **Subsonic (Ma < 1)** | Flow **accelerates** toward sonic; pressure **drops** | Max length $L_{\text{max}}$ to reach Ma = 1; beyond that → choking |
| **Supersonic (Ma > 1)** | Flow **decelerates** toward sonic; pressure **rises** | Max length $L_{\text{max}}$ to reach Ma = 1; beyond → choke + possible shock |

> ⚠️ **Counter-intuitive:** Friction **accelerates** subsonic flow! This is because friction heats the gas, and the energy addition primarily increases kinetic energy.

### A6. Fanno Flow — Operational Limits

- If the actual duct length $L > L_{\text{max}}$ for the given inlet conditions, the flow **chokes** (Ma = 1 at exit)
- If supersonic inlet flow encounters too long a duct, a **normal shock** forms inside the duct to allow the flow to reach the exit condition
- Fanno tables give $\frac{4fL_{\text{max}}}{D}$, $p/p^*$, $T/T^*$, $V/V^*$, $p_0/p_0^*$ as functions of Ma

## Part B: Rayleigh Flow (Constant Area, No Friction, Heat Transfer)

### B1. Physical Picture

Flow through a constant-area duct with heat addition or removal (through walls), but no friction. The assumptions: steady, 1-D, constant area, ideal gas, negligible friction.

### B2. Governing Equations

| Law | Equation |
|-----|----------|
| **Continuity** | $\rho V = \text{const}$ |
| **Momentum (no friction)** | $p + \rho V^2 = \text{const}$ (Euler + constant area) |
| **Energy** | $q = c_p(T_{02} - T_{01})$ — heat transfer changes stagnation temperature |
| **Ideal gas** | $p = \rho RT$ |

### B3. Rayleigh Line on $h$-$s$ Diagram

The set of states satisfying continuity and momentum for a given mass velocity and specific impulse lies on the **Rayleigh line**:

- **Subsonic branch:** heating (adding $q$) moves flow toward Ma = 1
- **Supersonic branch:** heating moves flow toward Ma = 1
- **Maximum entropy point:** occurs at Ma = 1

> 📌 **Heating always moves the Mach number toward 1**, whether starting from subsonic or supersonic. Cooling moves it away from 1.

### B4. Rayleigh Flow Relations

**Stagnation temperature ratio:**
$$\frac{T_0}{T_0^*} = \frac{2(k+1)\,\text{Ma}^2\,(1 + \frac{k-1}{2}\text{Ma}^2)}{(1 + k\,\text{Ma}^2)^2} \qquad \text{(11.98)}$$

**Stagnation pressure ratio:**
$$\frac{p_0}{p_0^*} = \frac{k+1}{1 + k\,\text{Ma}^2} \left[\frac{2(1 + \frac{k-1}{2}\text{Ma}^2)}{k+1}\right]^{\frac{k}{k-1}} \qquad \text{(11.99)}$$

Where $T_0^*$ and $p_0^*$ are the stagnation conditions at the sonic state (Ma = 1).

### B5. Rayleigh Behavior Summary

| Process | Subsonic | Supersonic |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **Heating ($dT_0 > 0$)** | Ma ↑ (toward 1), $p$ ↓, $T$ can ↑ or ↓ | Ma ↓ (toward 1), $p$ ↑, $T$ can ↑ or ↓ |
| **Cooling ($dT_0 < 0$)** | Ma ↓ (away from 1), $p$ ↑ | Ma ↑ (away from 1), $p$ ↓ |

> ⚠️ **Another counter-intuitive result:** In supersonic Rayleigh flow, **heating can decrease static temperature!** Below a certain Ma, the temperature on the Rayleigh line decreases as heat is added.

### B6. Rayleigh Choking

- Maximum heat addition for a given inlet Ma = heat required to reach Ma = 1 at duct exit
- Adding more heat than this limit → the flow chokes (Ma = 1 at exit), and the heat addition pattern adjusts upstream

## Part C: Fanno vs Rayleigh Comparison

| Feature | Fanno Flow | Rayleigh Flow |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| **Driving mechanism** | Wall friction | Heat transfer |
| **Adiabatic?** | Yes | No |
| **Frictionless?** | No | Yes |
| **Constant stagnation temperature?** | Yes ($T_0$ const) | No ($T_0$ changes with heat transfer) |
| **Constant mass velocity?** | $G = \rho V$ varies | $G$ const |
| **Choking condition** | Duct too long for given Ma | Too much heat addition |
| **Subsonic: effect on Ma** | Increases toward 1 | Increases toward 1 (heating) |
| **Supersonic: effect on Ma** | Decreases toward 1 | Decreases toward 1 (heating) |

Despite different physics, both Fanno and Rayleigh flows **drive the Mach number toward 1** in a constant-area duct.

---

## Part D: Combined Fanno-Rayleigh Flow

Real combustors (e.g., ramjet, scramjet) have **both friction and heat addition**. The combined analysis uses:

$$\frac{d\text{Ma}^2}{\text{Ma}^2} = \frac{1 + k\text{Ma}^2}{1 - \text{Ma}^2}\left(\frac{dT_0}{T_0}\right) + \frac{k\text{Ma}^2(1 + \frac{k-1}{2}\text{Ma}^2)}{1 - \text{Ma}^2}\left(\frac{4fdx}{D}\right)$$

In practice, engineers use numerical integration or tabulated values for combined effects.

## Key Takeaways

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| Friction accelerates subsonic flow | Opposite to incompressible intuition — important for gas pipeline design |
| Both Fanno & Rayleigh flow drive Ma → 1 | Neither can produce a smooth transition from subsonic to supersonic in constant area |
| Rayleigh heating can decrease $T$ | Supersonic combustor design must account for this |
| Choking is a universal limit | Once Ma = 1 is reached, downstream changes cannot propagate upstream |
| Tables are practical tools | Fanno and Rayleigh tables provide $\frac{4fL_{\text{max}}}{D}$, $T_0/T_0^*$, $p_0/p_0^*$, etc. by Ma |
