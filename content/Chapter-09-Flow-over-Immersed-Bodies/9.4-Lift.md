# Section 9.4: Lift

> **Core theme:** Lift is the fluid force component perpendicular to the upstream flow — most importantly, the force that keeps airplanes in the air. It arises primarily from an asymmetric pressure distribution around the body, which in turn depends on the body shape (camber), orientation (angle of attack), and the presence of circulation. For finite wings, lift comes with an inevitable penalty — induced drag.

***

## 1. Airfoil Geometry and Terminology

### 1.1 Basic Airfoil Parameters

| Term | Symbol | Definition |
|------|--------|------------|
| **Chord length** | $c$ | Straight-line distance from leading edge to trailing edge |
| **Camber line** | — | Midline between upper and lower surfaces |
| **Maximum camber** | $h$ | Max deviation of camber line from chord line |
| **Thickness** | $t$ | Max distance between upper and lower surfaces |
| **Angle of attack** | $\alpha$ | Angle between chord line and upstream velocity $U$ |

### 1.2 Symmetric vs Cambered Airfoils

| Feature | Symmetric Airfoil | Cambered Airfoil |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Camber line | = chord line (straight) | Curved |
| Lift at $\alpha = 0$ | Zero | Positive (upper surface flow faster) |
| Uses | Aerobatic planes, control surfaces | Commercial aircraft, efficiency-oriented |

***

## 2. Lift Generation Mechanism

### 2.1 Pressure Distribution

Lift is primarily a **pressure force**. From Eq. (9.2):

$$\mathcal{L} = -\int p\sin\theta\,dA + \int \tau_w\cos\theta\,dA$$

The shear term is typically small for lift — **pressure dominates**.

For a lifting airfoil at positive $\alpha$:

| Surface | Flow Speed (relative to wing) | Pressure (Bernoulli) |
|---------|-------------------------------|----------------------|
| Upper | Faster (longer path) | Lower |
| Lower | Slower | Higher |

→ Net upward pressure imbalance → **lift**

### 2.2 The Stagnation Point

- The upstream flow divides at the **stagnation point** on the leading edge
- For a symmetric airfoil at $\alpha = 0$: stagnation point at leading edge → symmetric flow → zero lift
- For $\alpha > 0$: stagnation point moves to **lower surface** of leading edge → flow must go around the leading edge → accelerates over upper surface → lower pressure

### 2.3 Lift Coefficient

$$C_L = \frac{\mathcal{L}}{\frac12 \rho U^2 A}$$

where $A$ is the **planform area** (wing area = span × chord for rectangular wings).

## 3. Circulation and the Kutta-Joukowski Theorem

### 3.1 The Concept of Circulation

$$\Gamma = \oint \mathbf{V}\cdot d\mathbf{s}$$

Circulation $\Gamma$ is the line integral of velocity around a closed curve enclosing the airfoil.

For a lifting airfoil $\Gamma \neq 0$ — the flow has a net "rotation" around the wing.

### 3.2 Kutta-Joukowski Lift Theorem

$$\mathcal{L} = \rho U \Gamma$$

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| $\rho$ | Fluid density |
| $U$ | Free-stream velocity |
| $\Gamma$ | Circulation around the airfoil (per unit span) |

> 📌 This is a **key result of inviscid flow theory**: lift per unit span is directly proportional to circulation. The theorem is exact for 2D inviscid, incompressible flow.

### 3.3 Generation of Circulation: The Kutta Condition

1. In real viscous flow, the rear stagnation point must be at the **sharp trailing edge** (this is the **Kutta condition**)
2. This forces the upper-surface flow to leave smoothly at the trailing edge
3. A starting vortex is shed when the wing begins motion
4. The bound vortex (circulation around the wing) remains as a consequence

> 💡 **Physical picture:** The Kutta condition resolves d'Alembert's paradox by selecting the unique inviscid solution that matches real viscous behavior — ensuring finite circulation and thus finite lift.

### 3.4 Lift on a Symmetric Airfoil (Thin Airfoil Theory)

For a symmetric airfoil at small $\alpha$ (thin airfoil theory):

$$C_L = 2\pi\alpha \qquad\text{(per radian)}$$

or $C_L = 0.11\alpha$ (per degree).

| $\alpha$ (degrees) | $C_L$ (thin airfoil theory) |
|------|------------------------------|
| $0^\circ$ | 0 |
| $5^\circ$ | 0.55 |
| $10^\circ$ | 1.10 |
| $15^\circ$ | 1.65 |

> ⚠️ This linear relationship holds only for moderate $\alpha$ (up to $\sim 10^\circ{-}15^\circ$ before stall).

For a cambered airfoil:

$$C_L = 2\pi(\alpha - \alpha_{L=0})$$

where $\alpha_{L=0}$ is the angle of attack for zero lift (negative for positive camber).

## 4. Stalling

### 4.1 What Happens at Stall

| $\alpha$ regime | Flow behavior | $C_L$ |
|----------------|---------------|-------|
| Small $\alpha$ ($<10^\circ$) | Flow attached, lift increases linearly | Rising |
| Moderate $\alpha$ ($\sim 12^\circ{-}15^\circ$) | Flow separation starts near trailing edge | Sub-linear |
| **Stall angle** $\alpha_{\text{stall}}$ | Massive separation on upper surface | **Peak $C_L$** |
| Post-stall ($\alpha > \alpha_{\text{stall}}$) | Fully separated flow over upper surface | **Sharp drop in $C_L$** |

### 4.2 Why Stall Happens

- At high $\alpha$, the upper surface flow must decelerate strongly near the leading edge and then accelerate again
- The adverse pressure gradient on the rear of the upper surface becomes too steep
- The BL cannot overcome it → **separation**
- The wake grows dramatically → lift collapses, drag surges

### 4.3 Factors Affecting Stall

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Airfoil thickness | Thicker airfoils stall at higher $\alpha$ |
| Camber | More camber = higher $C_{L,\max}$ but earlier stall? (depends on design) |
| Reynolds number | Higher Re → later stall (turbulent BL resists separation) |
| Surface roughness | Roughness on leading edge can trigger early stall |
| **Flaps and slats** | Extend the linear $C_L$ range and increase $C_{L,\max}$ |

> 💡 **Practical consequence:** Stall is one of the most dangerous flight conditions. Aircraft are designed to stall at speeds safely above landing speed, and stall warning systems alert pilots before separation occurs.

## 5. Drag on Wings: Induced Drag

### 5.1 The Problem with Finite Wings

For a **finite span** wing:
- Pressure difference between lower (high p) and upper (low p) surfaces
- Near the wingtips, high-pressure air **leaks** around the tip to the low-pressure side
- This creates **trailing vortices** (wingtip vortices)
- The trailing vortices induce a **downwash** — a downward velocity component behind the wing

### 5.2 Induced Drag

The downwash tilts the effective lift vector backward, producing a drag component:

$$\mathcal{D}_i = \mathcal{L}\sin\alpha_i \approx \mathcal{L}\,\alpha_i$$

where $\alpha_i$ is the **induced angle of attack** (downwash angle).

$$C_{D,i} = \frac{C_L^2}{\pi AR}$$

where $AR = b^2/A_{\text{wing}}$ = **aspect ratio** ($b$ = span, $A_{\text{wing}}$ = planform area).

| Aspect Ratio | Example | $C_{D,i}$ at $C_L=1.0$ |
|-------------|---------|----------------------|
| High ($AR \approx 20$) | Glider | Low induced drag |
| Moderate ($AR \approx 7$) | Commercial jet | Moderate |
| Low ($AR \approx 3$) | Fighter jet, paper airplane | High induced drag |

### 5.3 Total Drag on a Wing

$$C_D = C_{D,0} + C_{D,i} = C_{D,0} + \frac{C_L^2}{\pi AR}$$

where $C_{D,0}$ = profile drag (friction + pressure drag of the 2D airfoil section).

### 5.4 Lift-to-Drag Ratio

$$L/D = \frac{C_L}{C_D} = \frac{C_L}{C_{D,0} + C_L^2/(\pi AR)}$$

| Aspect | Importance |
|--------|-----------|
| Maximum $L/D$ | Occurs at a specific $C_L$ (optimal cruise condition) |
| Higher $AR$ | Higher $L/D$ (more efficient — but heavier wing structure) |
| Surface finish | Low $C_{D,0}$ = higher overall $L/D$ |

> 📌 **Typical $L/D$ values:** Bicycle+rider: $\sim 1$; car: $\sim 3{-}4$; commercial jet in cruise: $\sim 15{-}20$; albatross (gliding): $\sim 20$; high-performance glider: $\sim 50$ (incredible efficiency!).

## 6. Effects of Compressibility and Other Factors

### 6.1 Mach Number Effects

- For $\text{Ma} < 0.3$: incompressible flow assumed, no correction needed
- For $\text{Ma} > 0.3$: **Prandtl-Glauert correction** for subsonic flow:
  $$C_L = \frac{C_{L,\text{inc}}}{\sqrt{1 - \text{Ma}^2}}$$
- At transonic speeds ($\text{Ma} \approx 0.8{-}1.2$): local supersonic regions, shock waves → **wave drag** added
- Supersonic: very different lift/drag characteristics (thin wings, swept leading edges)

### 6.2 Ground Effect

- Flying close to ground ($h < \text{span}$): downwash is reduced
- **Induced drag decreases**
- Aircraft "floats" during landing flare
- Why geese fly in V formation — the wingtip vortices of the leader reduce induced drag for the follower!

## Key Takeaways

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| Lift is from **pressure imbalance** across upper/lower surfaces | Lower pressure on top → upward force |
| Circulation $\Gamma$ and Kutta-Joukowski $\mathcal{L} = \rho U\Gamma$ | Mathematical link: lift requires circulation |
| Thin airfoil theory: $C_L \approx 2\pi\alpha$ | Simple linear relation — but only pre-stall |
| **Stall** = flow separation → lift collapse | The fundamental limit on lifting ability |
| Finite wings produce **induced drag** $C_{D,i} = C_L^2/(\pi AR)$ | Lift always comes with a drag penalty for finite wings |
| Higher aspect ratio = lower induced drag | Why gliders have long, narrow wings |
