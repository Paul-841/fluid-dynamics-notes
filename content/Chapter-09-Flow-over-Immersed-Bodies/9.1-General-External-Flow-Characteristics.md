# Section 9.1: General External Flow Characteristics

> **Core theme:** External flows are characterized by a body fully immersed in a moving fluid. The body experiences forces through integrated pressure and shear stress distributions. Key decisions: coordinate system (fixed to body), body classification (2D/axisymmetric/3D, streamlined/blunt), and the decomposition of the resultant force into lift and drag.

***

## 1. Coordinate System

For external flows, we fix the coordinate system to the body:

- **Body stationary, fluid moving:** wind past a building — $U$ is the upstream velocity
- **Body moving, fluid stationary:** airplane flying through still air — $U$ is the body velocity
- **Either way:** mathematically identical; $U$ = upstream (free-stream) velocity
- Assume **uniform, steady** upstream flow for analysis (though real flows are often gusty)

***

## 2. Body Classification

### 2.1 Geometric Classification

Three categories (Fig 9.2):

| Type | Example | Characteristic |
|------|---------|---------------|
| **Two-dimensional** (infinite perpendicular to page) | Infinite cylinder, airfoil span $\gg$ chord | End effects negligible; only 2 coordinates needed |
| **Axisymmetric** (rotated about symmetry axis) | Bullet, submarine, sphere | Equivalent to 2D flow in $(r,x)$ coordinates |
| **Three-dimensional** | Automobile, finite wing, building | Full 3D flow; most complex |

> ⚠️ Truly 2D bodies don't exist, but many are long enough for end effects to be negligible.

### 2.2 Streamlined vs Blunt

| Streamlined | Blunt |
|-------------|-------|
| Minimal disturbance to flow (airfoils, racing cars) | Large disturbance (parachutes, buildings) |
| Low pressure drag | High pressure drag |
| Flow follows surface for most of body | Early flow separation |
| Usually easier to push through fluid | Usually harder (with exceptions) |

***

## 3. Lift and Drag Concepts

### 3.1 Forces from Fluid-Body Interaction

The fluid exerts two types of stress on the body surface:

| Stress | Symbol | Origin | Direction |
|--------|--------|--------|-----------|
| **Pressure** (normal) | $p$ | Normal force per area | Always normal to surface |
| **Shear** (tangential) | $\tau_w$ | Viscous friction | Always tangential to surface |

Both $p$ and $\tau_w$ vary in magnitude and direction along the surface.

### 3.2 Drag and Lift Definitions

| Force | Direction | Symbol |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| **Drag** $\mathcal{D}$ | Parallel to upstream velocity $U$ | $\mathcal{D}$ |
| **Lift** $\mathcal{L}$ | Perpendicular to upstream velocity $U$ | $\mathcal{L}$ |

> 📌 For 3D bodies, a **side force** may also exist (perpendicular to both $\mathcal{D}$ and $\mathcal{L}$).

### 3.3 Force Integration (Figs 9.3–9.4)

For a small area element $dA$ with surface orientation angle $\theta$:

$$dF_x = (p\,dA)\cos\theta + (\tau_w\,dA)\sin\theta$$
$$dF_y = -(p\,dA)\sin\theta + (\tau_w\,dA)\cos\theta$$

Integrating over the entire body surface:

$$\mathcal{D} = \int dF_x = \int p\cos\theta\,dA + \int \tau_w\sin\theta\,dA \qquad\text{(9.1)}$$

$$\mathcal{L} = \int dF_y = -\int p\sin\theta\,dA + \int \tau_w\cos\theta\,dA \qquad\text{(9.2)}$$

| Term in (9.1) | Physical Meaning |
|---------------|------------------|
| $\int p\cos\theta\,dA$ | Pressure contribution to drag **(form/pressure drag)** |
| $\int \tau_w\sin\theta\,dA$ | Shear contribution to drag **(friction drag)** |

| Term in (9.2) | Physical Meaning |
|---------------|------------------|
| $-\int p\sin\theta\,dA$ | Pressure contribution to lift |
| $\int \tau_w\cos\theta\,dA$ | Shear contribution to lift (usually small) |

> ⚠️ **Both stresses contribute to both lift and drag** for arbitrary body shapes — $\theta$ is neither $0^\circ$ nor $90^\circ$ everywhere unless the body is a flat plate aligned with or normal to the flow.

### 3.4 Pressure-Sensitive Paint (PSP)

Modern alternative to static pressure taps:

| Traditional taps | PSP |
|-----------------|-----|
| Discrete point measurements | Global (entire surface) |
| Small holes disturb surface | Non-intrusive |
| Many tubes required | Single paint + camera |
| Time-consuming, costly | Faster and cheaper |

## 4. Example 9.1: Flat Plate — Drag from Stress Distributions

### Case (a): Plate parallel to flow ($\theta = 90^\circ$)

The plate surface is everywhere parallel to $U$:
- Normal stress (pressure): only side-on surfaces have finite area in the $x$-direction → $p$ on the leading edge area → negligible if plate is thin
- Shear stress: $\tau_w$ acts on the top and bottom surfaces in the $x$-direction

For a plate of length $L$ and width $b$ (one side):
$$\mathcal{D} = \int_0^L \tau_w(x) \cdot b\,dx$$

Since $\cos 90^\circ = 0$ and $\sin 90^\circ = 1$ in Eq 9.1, drag is **entirely from shear** — this is pure **friction drag**.
Lift is zero by symmetry (if plate is symmetric and at zero angle of attack).

### Case (b): Plate normal to flow ($\theta = 0^\circ$)

The plate surface is everywhere perpendicular to $U$:
- Normal stress (pressure) acts directly in the $x$-direction
- Shear stress acts perpendicular to the plate → contributes no $x$-component

$$\mathcal{D} = \int_A p\,dA$$

Since $\cos 0^\circ = 1$ and $\sin 0^\circ = 1$ in Eq 9.1:
- The $p\cos\theta$ term becomes $\int p\,dA$ = pure **pressure drag**
- The $\tau_w\sin\theta$ term: $\tau_w$ contributes to **lift** (try to pull plate sideways), not drag

Lift is zero by symmetry (equal pressure on both halves).

> 📌 **Key Insight:** The same body — a flat plate — has drag from entirely different mechanisms depending on its orientation. This illustrates why external flow analysis must consider both pressure and shear contributions.
