# Section 9.2: Boundary Layer Characteristics

> **Core theme:** The boundary layer (Prandtl, 1904) is the thin region near a body surface where viscous effects are concentrated. Outside the BL, the flow is essentially inviscid. Inside, velocity gradients are large, and shear stress is significant. The BL determines **friction drag** and sets the stage for **separation**, which controls pressure drag. For a flat plate (zero pressure gradient), exact and empirical solutions give $\delta(x)$, $\tau_w(x)$, and $c_f(x)$.

***

## 1. The Boundary Layer Concept

| Outside BL (free-stream) | Inside BL |
|--------------------------|-----------|
| Inviscid (Euler eqns) | Viscous (NS eqns, simplified) |
| $u(x,y) \approx U(x)$ | $u(x,y)$ goes from 0 at wall to $U$ at edge |
| No-slip doesn't matter | No-slip is central ($u=0$ at wall) |
| Velocity gradients negligible | Large $\partial u/\partial y$ → high $\tau_w$ |

### Key Transition Points

For a flat plate in a steady uniform stream:

| Regime | $\text{Re}_x = Ux/\nu$ | Characteristics |
|--------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Laminar BL** | $\text{Re}_x \lesssim 5\times10^5$ | Smooth, $\delta \propto x^{1/2}$, parabolic-like profile |
| **Transition** | $5\times10^5 \lesssim \text{Re}_x \lesssim 3\times10^6$ | Tollmien–Schlichting waves → 3D breakdown → turbulent spots |
| **Turbulent BL** | $\text{Re}_x \gtrsim 3\times10^6$ | Chaotic, $\delta \propto x^{4/5}$, fuller velocity profile |

> ⚠️ Transition depends on free-stream turbulence, surface roughness, pressure gradient, and wall heating/cooling. The $5\times10^5$ value is for a smooth flat plate in low-turbulence air.

***

## 2. Boundary Layer Thickness Definitions

| Thickness | Symbol | Definition | Physical Meaning |
|-----------|--------|------------|------------------|
| **Nominal thickness** | $\delta$ | $y$ where $u = 0.99U$ | Standard engineering measure |
| **Displacement thickness** | $\delta^*$ | $\displaystyle \int_0^\infty\!\left(1 - \frac{u}{U}\right)dy$ | How far streamlines are "pushed outward" by the BL |
| **Momentum thickness** | $\theta$ | $\displaystyle \int_0^\infty \frac{u}{U}\!\left(1 - \frac{u}{U}\right)dy$ | Momentum deficit due to BL |
| **Shape factor** | $H$ | $H = \delta^*/\theta$ | Laminar $H \approx 2.6$, turbulent $H \approx 1.4$ |

### Physical Interpretation of $\delta^*$

The displacement thickness tells us how much the BL **displaces** the external flow:
- Inviscid flow past a body "sees" a body that is thicker by $\delta^*$
- For external flows, the effective body shape = actual body + $\delta^*(x)$ boundary layer growth
- For internal flows (Ch08), $\delta^*$ relates to the displacement effect of the entrance region

### Why Shape Factor Matters

$H = \delta^*/\theta$ is a diagnostic:
- $H \approx 2.6$ (laminar) → profile more "skinny" — more susceptible to separation
- $H \approx 1.4$ (turbulent) → profile more "full" — resists separation better
- **Rising $H$** in a laminar BL signals impending separation

***

## 3. Laminar Boundary Layer: Blasius Exact Solution (1908)

### 3.1 Setup

For steady, incompressible, laminar flow over a flat plate at zero incidence:
- $U = \text{constant}$ (zero pressure gradient: $dU/dx = 0$)
- No-slip at wall: $u(x,0) = 0$, $v(x,0) = 0$
- Free-stream: $u(x,\infty) = U$

### 3.2 Similarity Transformation

Blasius introduced the similarity variable:
$$\eta = y\sqrt{\frac{U}{\nu x}}$$

and the stream function $\psi = \sqrt{\nu U x}\,f(\eta)$, where $f$ satisfies:

$$2f''' + f f'' = 0 \qquad\text{(Blasius equation)}$$
$$f(0) = 0,\quad f'(0) = 0,\quad f'(\infty) = 1$$

> 📌 The Blasius equation is a **nonlinear ODE** solved numerically. No closed-form solution exists.

### 3.3 Key Results

| Quantity | Formula | Notes |
|----------|---------|-------|
| Velocity profile | $u/U = f'(\eta)$ | Universal function of $\eta$ alone |
| BL thickness | $\displaystyle \delta \approx \frac{5.0x}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}}$ | At $\eta \approx 5.0$, $u/U = 0.99$ |
| Displacement thickness | $\displaystyle \delta^* = \frac{1.721x}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}}$ | $\delta^*/\delta \approx 0.344$ |
| Momentum thickness | $\displaystyle \theta = \frac{0.664x}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}}$ | $H = \delta^*/\theta \approx 2.59$ |
| Wall shear stress | $\displaystyle \tau_w(x) = \frac{0.332\mu U\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}}{x}$ | $f''(0) = 0.332$ |
| Local skin friction coeff | $\displaystyle c_f(x) = \frac{\tau_w}{\frac12\rho U^2} = \frac{0.664}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}}$ | $\text{Re}_x = Ux/\nu$ |
| Total drag (one side) | $\displaystyle \mathcal{D}_f = b\int_0^L \tau_w(x)\,dx$ | Integrate over plate |
| Total drag coefficient | $\displaystyle C_{D_f} = \frac{\mathcal{D}_f}{\frac12\rho U^2(bL)} = \frac{1.328}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}}$ | $\text{Re}_L = UL/\nu$ |

### 3.4 Scaling Observations

| $\text{Re}_x$ | $\delta/x$ | $\tau_w$ |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| $10^4$ | 0.050 | Highest (leading edge) |
| $10^5$ | 0.016 | Decreasing |
| $5\times10^5$ | 0.007 | Lowest before transition |

- $\delta \propto \sqrt{x}$ — BL grows parabolically
- $\tau_w \propto 1/\sqrt{x}$ — shear is highest at leading edge, decays downstream

## 4. Turbulent Boundary Layer

### 4.1 General Characteristics

| Property | Laminar BL | Turbulent BL |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Velocity profile shape | Parabolic-like (low near-wall momentum) | Fuller (higher near-wall momentum) |
| $\delta(x)$ growth | $\propto x^{1/2}$ | $\propto x^{4/5}$ (faster growth) |
| $\tau_w(x)$ | $\propto x^{-1/2}$ (decays) | $\propto x^{-1/5}$ (decays slower) |
| Wall shear | Lower | **Higher** (3–10× laminar) |
| Separation resistance | Weak | Strong (fuller profile) |

### 4.2 Velocity Profile: 1/7th Power Law (Empirical)

For moderate Reynolds numbers ($\text{Re}_x < 10^7$):
$$\frac{u}{U} = \left(\frac{y}{\delta}\right)^{1/7}$$

> ⚠️ This is a **curve-fit**, not a fundamental law. It fails at $y=0$ (predicts infinite $du/dy$) and is only valid in the overlap/inertial sublayer region.

### 4.3 Key Results (Empirical)

| Quantity | Formula | Range |
|----------|---------|-------|
| BL thickness | $\displaystyle \delta \approx \frac{0.37x}{\text{Re}_x^{1/5}}$ | $5\times10^5 < \text{Re}_x < 10^7$ |
| Wall shear | $\displaystyle \tau_w(x) \approx \frac{0.0296\rho U^2}{\text{Re}_x^{1/5}}$ | From 1/7th power law |
| Local skin friction | $\displaystyle c_f(x) = \frac{0.0592}{\text{Re}_x^{1/5}}$ | $5\times10^5 < \text{Re}_x < 10^7$ |
| Total drag coeff (one side) | $\displaystyle C_{D_f} = \frac{0.074}{\text{Re}_L^{1/5}}$ | $5\times10^5 < \text{Re}_L < 10^7$ |
| High-Re correlation | $\displaystyle C_{D_f} = \frac{0.455}{(\log_{10}\text{Re}_L)^{2.58}}$ | Schlichting, $\text{Re}_L > 10^7$ |
| Displacement thickness | $\displaystyle \delta^* = \frac{0.046x}{\text{Re}_x^{1/5}}$ | Using 1/7th law ($H \approx 1.3$) |
| Momentum thickness | $\displaystyle \theta = \frac{0.036x}{\text{Re}_x^{1/5}}$ | Using 1/7th law |

***

## 5. Momentum-Integral Boundary Layer Equation (von Kármán)

### 5.1 Derivation Idea

Integrate the boundary layer equations from $y=0$ to $y = \delta$ to eliminate dependence on $y$, producing an ODE in $x$:

$$\frac{\tau_w}{\rho U^2} = \frac{d\theta}{dx} + \frac{(2+H)\theta}{U}\frac{dU}{dx} \qquad\text{(von Kármán integral equation)}$$

where $U = U(x)$ is the free-stream velocity at the edge of the BL.

### 5.2 Why This Is Powerful

| Feature | Benefit |
|---------|---------|
| **Works for any BL** (laminar, turbulent, pressure gradient) | Universal tool — no restriction to flat plate |
| **$U(x)$ can vary** | Handles real airfoils, diffusers, etc. |
| **Approximate method** | Assumes a velocity profile shape $u/U = g(y/\delta)$ |
| **Accurate enough** | Usually within a few percent of exact solutions |

### 5.3 Application Procedure

1. Assume a velocity profile shape: $u/U = f(\eta)$ where $\eta = y/\delta(x)$
2. Compute $\delta^*$, $\theta$, $\tau_w$ in terms of $\delta(x)$
3. Plug into von Kármán equation → solve for $\delta(x)$
4. Then find $\tau_w(x)$ and $c_f(x)$ from $\delta(x)$

### 5.4 Example: Flat Plate ($U =$ const, $dU/dx = 0$)

The von Kármán equation simplifies to:
$$\frac{\tau_w}{\rho U^2} = \frac{d\theta}{dx}$$

For the Blasius profile $\left(\frac{u}{U} = 2\eta - \eta^2 + \cdots\right)$:
- $\theta = C_1\delta$ (proportional to $\delta$)
- $\tau_w = \mu U C_2/\delta$

Substituting gives: $\delta \propto \sqrt{x}$ — recovering the Blasius scaling without solving the full PDE!

## 6. Transition from Laminar to Turbulent

### 6.1 Physical Mechanism

1. **Instability:** Small disturbances in laminar BL grow as Tollmien–Schlichting (T-S) waves
2. **Nonlinear breakdown:** 2D waves become 3D, develop hairpin vortices
3. **Turbulent spots:** Local patches of turbulence appear and spread
4. **Fully turbulent:** Spots merge → continuous turbulent BL

### 6.2 Factors Affecting Transition

| Factor | Effect on Transition $\text{Re}_x$ |
|--------|----------------------------------|
| Increasing free-stream turbulence | **Lowers** $\text{Re}_{\text{crit}}$ (earlier transition) |
| Surface roughness | **Lowers** $\text{Re}_{\text{crit}}$ |
| Favorable pressure gradient ($dp/dx < 0$) | **Raises** $\text{Re}_{\text{crit}}$ (stabilizes BL) |
| Adverse pressure gradient ($dp/dx > 0$) | **Lowers** $\text{Re}_{\text{crit}}$ (destabilizes) |
| Wall heating (gas) | **Lowers** $\text{Re}_{\text{crit}}$ |
| Wall suction | **Raises** $\text{Re}_{\text{crit}}$ (stabilizes, can relaminarize) |

### 6.3 Engineering Approach

Unless specified otherwise:
- Use laminar from leading edge to $\text{Re}_x = 5\times10^5$
- Use turbulent from there onward
- This is the standard "mixed BL" calculation

### 6.4 Mixed Boundary Layer Drag

For a flat plate with transition at $\text{Re}_{x_t}$:

$$C_{D_f} = \frac{1}{\text{Re}_L}\!\left(\int_0^{\text{Re}_{x_t}} c_{f,\text{lam}}\,d\text{Re}_x + \int_{\text{Re}_{x_t}}^{\text{Re}_L} c_{f,\text{turb}}\,d\text{Re}_x\right)$$

For transition at $\text{Re}_{x_t} = 5\times10^5$:

$$C_{D_f} = \frac{0.074}{\text{Re}_L^{1/5}} - \frac{1740}{\text{Re}_L}$$

> 📌 The $-1740/\text{Re}_L$ term subtracts the "turbulent BL from leading edge" overcount and replaces it with the laminar contribution.

***

## Key Takeaways

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| BL thickness $\delta \propto x^{1/2}$ (lam), $x^{4/5}$ (turb) | Explains growth behavior |
| $\tau_w \propto 1/\sqrt{x}$ (lam), $1/x^{1/5}$ (turb) | Higher shear near leading edge, but turbulent shear dominates |
| $C_{D_f} \propto 1/\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}$ (lam), $\propto 1/\text{Re}_L^{1/5}$ (turb) | Turbulent drag decreases slower with $\text{Re}$ |
| $H \approx 2.6$ (lam) vs $1.4$ (turb) | Turbulent BL resists separation better |
| von Kármán integral works for any $U(x)$ | Enables analysis of realistic geometries |
