# Section 8.1: General Characteristics of Pipe Flow

> **Core theme:** Before analyzing pipe flow quantitatively, we need to understand the fundamental distinctions: pipe flow vs open-channel flow, laminar vs turbulent regimes, and the concept of the entrance region where the flow "develops."

***

## 1. Pipe Flow vs Open-Channel Flow

| Pipe Flow (Ch08) | Open-Channel Flow (Ch10) |
|------------------|--------------------------|
| Pipe is **completely filled** with fluid | Free surface exposed to atmosphere |
| Driving force: **pressure gradient** $\Delta p$ along pipe | Driving force: **gravity** (fluid flows downhill) |
| $p_1 \neq p_2$ | $p_1 = p_2$ (at free surface) |
| Examples: water mains, oil pipelines, blood vessels | Examples: rivers, canals, storm drains |

> 📌 If a pipe is not full, you cannot maintain a pressure difference $p_1 - p_2$ — the flow becomes open-channel flow.

***

## 2. Laminar, Transitional, and Turbulent Flow

### 2.1 Reynolds' Dye Experiment (1883)

Osborne Reynolds injected a thin dye streak into water flowing through a glass pipe:

| Flowrate (via $\text{Re}$) | Dye Behavior | Regime |
|---------------------------|-------------|--------|
| "Small enough" | Dye stays as a **well-defined line**, slight blurring from molecular diffusion only | **Laminar** |
| "Intermediate" | Dye streak **fluctuates**, intermittent bursts of irregular behavior | **Transitional** |
| "Large enough" | Dye **immediately disperses** across entire pipe cross-section | **Turbulent** |

### 2.2 The Reynolds Number Criterion

The distinction is governed by the **Reynolds number** — the ratio of inertia to viscous forces:

$$\text{Re} = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu} = \frac{V D}{\nu}$$

| $\text{Re}$ Range | Flow Regime |
|-------------------|-------------|
| $\text{Re} < 2100$ | **Laminar** — smooth, layered motion; $u = u(r)$ only |
| $2100 < \text{Re} < 4000$ | **Transitional** — unpredictable switching between laminar/turbulent |
| $\text{Re} > 4000$ | **Turbulent** — random 3D fluctuations: $\mathbf{V} = u\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + v\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} + w\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ |

> ⚠️ These are **engineering guidelines**. Actual transition depends on pipe vibrations, entrance smoothness, and upstream disturbances. With extreme care, laminar flow can be maintained to $\text{Re} \approx 100{,}000$.

> 💡 **Reynolds number first appeared in** [[../Chapter-07-Dimensional-Analysis-Similitude-and-Modeling/7.1-Need-for-Dimensional-Analysis\|Ch07 §7.1]] — the pipe flow problem was the original motivation for dimensional analysis!

### 2.3 Physical Difference

| Property | Laminar | Turbulent |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Velocity | $u(r)$ only (1 component) | $\mathbf{V} = (u, v, w)$ — unsteady, random |
| Mixing | Molecular diffusion only | Intense cross-stream mixing (eddies) |
| Velocity profile | Parabolic | "Fuller" (blunter) — higher near-wall gradient |
| Wall shear | Lower | Higher (hence larger pressure drop) |

***

## 3. Entrance Region and Fully Developed Flow

### 3.1 What Happens at the Entrance

When fluid enters a pipe from a reservoir:

1. **Entrance region:** Velocity profile changes from uniform (plug flow) toward its final shape
2. Boundary layers on pipe walls grow and eventually **merge** at the centerline
3. Beyond this point: **fully developed flow** — velocity profile no longer changes with $x$

### 3.2 Entrance Length

| Regime | Entrance Length $L_e$ |
|--------|----------------------|
| Laminar | $L_e/D \approx 0.06\,\text{Re}$ |
| Turbulent | $L_e/D \approx 4.4\,\text{Re}^{1/6}$ (≈ 10–60 diameters) |

For turbulent flow, $L_e$ is typically 10–60 pipe diameters. For laminar flow at $\text{Re} = 2000$, $L_e/D \approx 120$ — the entrance region can be very long!

***

## 4. Pressure Distribution in the Entrance Region

In the entrance region:
- Wall shear stress $\tau_w$ is **higher** than in the fully developed region
- Pressure gradient is **nonlinear** — the flow is still "developing"
- After entrance length: $dp/dx = \text{constant}$ (fully developed)

***

## Key Takeaways

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| $\text{Re} = \rho V D / \mu$ governs laminar vs turbulent | Determines which analysis method (exact vs experimental) applies |
| $\text{Re}_{\text{crit}} \approx 2100$ | The laminar/turbulent boundary for engineering design |
| Entrance region has higher $\tau_w$ | Must account for entrance effects in short pipes |
| Turbulent flow has 3D random fluctuations | Requires time-averaging (RANS approach) for analysis |
