# Section 8.3: Fully Developed Turbulent Flow in Pipes

> **Core theme:** Turbulence is the default state of most engineering pipe flows. Unlike laminar flow, there is no exact solution — we rely on dimensional analysis, experiments, and semi-empirical models to describe the velocity profile and predict wall shear.

***

## 1. Why Turbulence Is Hard

| Laminar | Turbulent |
|---------|-----------|
| $u(r)$ only — 1 velocity component | $\mathbf{V} = u\hat{\mathbf{i}} + v\hat{\mathbf{j}} + w\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ — instantaneous 3D, unsteady |
| Navier–Stokes reduces to ODE | Navier–Stokes is a 4D nonlinear PDE (chaotic) |
| Exact solution exists | No general analytical solution |

**The workaround:** Time-average the Navier–Stokes equations → **RANS** (Reynolds-Averaged Navier–Stokes). This introduces **Reynolds stresses** that must be modeled.

***

## 2. Time-Averaged Equations

Decompose velocity: $u = \bar{u} + u'$, where $\bar{u}$ = time-mean, $u'$ = fluctuation.

Time-averaging the $x$-momentum equation introduces the **Reynolds stress** term:

$$\tau_{\text{total}} = \mu\frac{d\bar{u}}{dr} \;-\; \rho\overline{u'v'}$$

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| $\mu\,d\bar{u}/dr$ | Viscous (laminar) shear stress |
| $-\rho\overline{u'v'}$ | Turbulent Reynolds stress — momentum transport by eddy mixing |

> 📌 In turbulent core: $-\rho\overline{u'v'} \gg \mu\,d\bar{u}/dr$ — turbulence dominates.

***

## 3. Structure of the Turbulent Velocity Profile

The turbulent boundary layer in a pipe has distinct layers:

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────┐
   Turbulent core   │  Logarithmic overlap     │ y⁺ ≳ 30
        │           ├─────────────────────────┤
        ▼           │  Buffer layer            │ 5 ≲ y⁺ ≲ 30
   ┌────────┐       ├─────────────────────────┤
   │  Viscous│       │  Viscous sublayer         │ y⁺ ≲ 5
   │ sublayer│       └─────────────────────────┘
   └────────┘
     WALL
```

### Dimensionless Variables

**Friction velocity:** $u^* = \sqrt{\tau_w/\rho}$

**Dimensionless distance from wall:** $y^+ = \frac{y u^*}{\nu}$ where $y = R - r$

**Dimensionless velocity:** $u^+ = \bar{u}/u^*$

***

## 4. Law of the Wall

### Viscous Sublayer ($y^+ \lesssim 5$)

Shear stress is essentially laminar: $\tau \approx \mu\,du/dy$

$$u^+ = y^+$$

### Logarithmic Overlap Layer ($y^+ \gtrsim 30$, $y/R \lesssim 0.2$)

Turbulence dominates; velocity follows a universal log law:

$$u^+ = \frac{1}{\kappa}\ln y^+ + B$$

where $\kappa \approx 0.41$ (von Kármán constant), $B \approx 5.0$ (smooth walls).

### Velocity Defect Law (Outer Layer)

For the core region, deficit from centerline:

$$\frac{V_c - \bar{u}}{u^*} = -\frac{1}{\kappa}\ln\!\left(\frac{y}{R}\right)$$

This is a similarity law — the same functional form works for all $\text{Re}$.

***

## 5. Turbulent Velocity Profile: Power-Law Approximation

For many engineering calculations, the 1/7th power law is adequate:

$$\frac{\bar{u}}{V_c} = \left(\frac{y}{R}\right)^{1/n}$$

| $\text{Re}$ | Exponent $n$ |
|------------|-------------|
| $4 \times 10^3$ | 6 |
| $1 \times 10^5$ | 7 |
| $3 \times 10^6$ | 10 |

The turbulent profile is **fuller** (blunter) than the parabolic laminar profile — $V/V_c \approx 0.8$ (vs 0.5 for laminar).

***

## Key Takeaways

| Layer | $y^+$ Range | Law |
|-------|------------|-----|
| Viscous sublayer | $< 5$ | $u^+ = y^+$ |
| Buffer | $5$–$30$ | Transition (no simple law) |
| Log layer | $> 30$ | $u^+ = \frac{1}{\kappa}\ln y^+ + B$ |
| Core | Outer | Defect law or power law |
