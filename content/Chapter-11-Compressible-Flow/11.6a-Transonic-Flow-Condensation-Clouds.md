# Supplement: Transonic Flow — Prandtl-Glauert Singularity & Condensation Clouds

> **Core theme:** The iconic "vapor cone" around aircraft at high subsonic speeds is the visible signature of **transonic flow** — a mixed subsonic/supersonic regime where linearized theory breaks down, local shock waves form, and isentropic cooling condenses atmospheric water vapor. This is a direct **physical consequence** of the normal shock theory and isentropic flow relations developed in previous sections.

$$ \text{Freestream }\text{Ma}_\infty \approx 0.7\text{–}0.95 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \text{Local supersonic bubble} + \text{Normal shock} + \text{Vapor cone} $$

## 2. The Prandtl-Glauert Singularity — Theory Breakdown at Mach 1

### 2.1 The PG Transformation

Before transonic flow was fully understood (1930s), engineers used the **Prandtl-Glauert (PG) transformation** to estimate compressibility effects on subsonic airfoils. The key result: the pressure coefficient $C_p$ on an airfoil at freestream Ma$_\infty$ relates to the incompressible value by:

$$C_p = \frac{C_{p,0}}{\sqrt{1 - \text{Ma}_\infty^2}}$$

where $C_{p,0}$ is the incompressible (Ma = 0) pressure coefficient.

### 2.2 The "Singularity"

As $\text{Ma}_\infty \to 1$, the denominator $\sqrt{1 - \text{Ma}_\infty^2} \to 0$, so $C_p \to \infty$. This is the **Prandtl-Glauert singularity** — a mathematical infinity in linearized theory.

### 2.3 What Actually Happens (Nature Prevents the Infinity)

The singularity is **not physical** — it reveals where linearized theory breaks down. In real flow:

1. As $\text{Ma}_\infty$ increases toward 1, flow over the curved upper surface of a wing accelerates to **locally supersonic speeds** ($\text{Ma}_{\text{local}} > 1$), even though the freestream is still subsonic.
2. A **local normal shock wave** forms at the end of this supersonic "bubble," decelerating the flow back to subsonic.
3. The shock limits the pressure coefficient to a finite value — the theoretical infinity never materializes.

> 📌 **The vapor cone is the visual proof** that linear PG theory has broken down and we have entered the nonlinear transonic regime with shock waves.

### 2.4 The Pitot Tube Connection

A similar singularity appears in Pitot tube measurements (Rayleigh-Pitot formula) without compressibility correction — which is why high-speed aircraft use compressible flow Pitot corrections based on normal shock theory.

## 3. The Condensation Cloud (Vapor Cone) — Step-by-Step Physics

This is a beautiful integration of **isentropic flow + thermodynamics + phase change**.

### Step 1: Flow Acceleration & Isentropic Expansion
- Air accelerates over the aircraft's curved surfaces (wing, fuselage, canopy).
- In transonic flight ($\text{Ma}_\infty \approx 0.7\text{–}0.95$), this local acceleration can push the flow to **supersonic** speeds.
- This expansion is essentially **isentropic** (adiabatic + frictionless in the inviscid core).

From the isentropic relations:

$$\frac{T}{T_0} = \frac{1}{1 + \frac{k-1}{2}\text{Ma}_{\text{local}}^2}$$

As $\text{Ma}_{\text{local}}$ increases, static temperature $T$ **drops sharply**.

### Step 2: Temperature Drops Below Dew Point
- Ambient air always contains water vapor.
- Saturation vapor pressure decreases **exponentially** with temperature (Clausius-Clapeyron relation).
- If local $T$ falls below the **dew point** of the ambient air, excess water vapor can no longer stay gaseous.

### Step 3: Condensation & Re-Evaporation
- Water vapor condenses into **microscopic liquid droplets** in the cold, low-pressure supersonic region.
- These droplets scatter sunlight — we see them as a **white cloud** (vapor cone).
- At the end of the supersonic bubble, flow crosses the **local normal shock wave**.
- Across the shock, $T$ and $p$ jump abruptly. The temperature spike instantly **evaporates** the droplets.
- Result: the cloud has a **sharp, well-defined trailing edge**.

> ✅ **The trailing edge of the condensation cloud = the exact location of the local normal shock wave.**

## 5. Common Misconceptions — Corrected

### ❌ "This is the aircraft breaking the sound barrier"
**No.** The vapor cone forms at *transonic* speeds ($\text{Ma}_\infty \lesssim 0.95$). The aircraft is still flying **slower than sound** relative to the undisturbed air ahead.
- "Breaking the sound barrier" = **fully supersonic** flight ($\text{Ma}_\infty > 1$).
- Vapor cone = **local** supersonic regions on the aircraft surface while freestream is still subsonic.

### ❌ "The cloud IS the shock wave"
**No.** The cloud forms in the **cold region ahead** of the shock. The shock is invisible — it's the thin line where the cloud's sharp trailing edge sits. The cloud = condensation; the shock is where the cloud ends.

### ❌ "Sonic booms are caused by this cloud"
**No.** These are separate phenomena:

| Phenomenon | Cause | Mach range |
|------------|-------|------------|
| **Vapor cone** | Isentropic cooling + condensation | Transonic ($\text{Ma}_\infty \approx 0.7$–0.95) |
| **Sonic boom** | Coalesced shock waves from fully supersonic aircraft | Supersonic ($\text{Ma}_\infty > 1$) |

Sonic booms require the entire aircraft to be supersonic. Airshows typically fly **transonic** passes (spectacular vapor cone, no broken windows).

### ❌ "The cloud always forms in supersonic flow"
**No.** Dry air → no condensation even if supersonic. The cloud requires:
1. Local supersonic flow (yes, always present in transonic)
2. Humid enough air that dew point is reached (weather-dependent)

## 6. Practical Significance

| Aspect | Implication |
|--------|-------------|
| **Drag rise** | Shock-induced drag (wave drag) increases sharply in transonic regime — the "sound barrier" is really a **drag barrier** |
| **Buffeting** | Shock oscillation can cause wing buffet |
| **Shock-boundary layer interaction** | Separation and performance loss |
| **Aerodynamic design** | Supercritical airfoils delay/shock weaken by reshaping the upper surface to reduce local Mach numbers |
