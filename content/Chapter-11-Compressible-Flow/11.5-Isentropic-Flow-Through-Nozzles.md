# Section 11.5: Isentropic Flow Through Nozzles

> **Core theme:** A nozzle converts thermal/enthalpy energy into kinetic energy. How it behaves depends on: (1) its geometry (converging vs. converging-diverging) and (2) the **back pressure** $p_b$ (pressure at nozzle exit). This section maps all possible operating regimes.

***

## 1. Converging Nozzle

### Setup
- Reservoir at $(p_0, T_0)$ with $V \approx 0$
- Nozzle exits into a region at back pressure $p_b$
- Throat at exit (minimum area)

### Operating Modes

| Regime | Condition | Exit Ma | Exit $p_e$ | Flow Character |
|--------|-----------|---------|------------|----------------|
| 1 | $p_b \ge p_0$ | — | — | No flow (or reverse flow) |
| 2 | $p_0 > p_b > p^*$ | Ma < 1 | $p_e = p_b$ | Subsonic throughout; mass flow increases as $p_b$ decreases |
| 3 | $p_b = p^*$ | Ma = 1 | $p_e = p^*$ | Sonic at exit; **choked** |
| 4 | $p_b < p^*$ | Ma = 1 | $p_e = p^* > p_b$ | Choked; supersonic flow would try to form but can't in a converging nozzle only; flow expands and compresses outside |

> 📌 **Choking threshold for air:** $p_b/p_0 = 0.528$. Below this ratio, the converging nozzle is choked — mass flow is constant regardless of further decreases in $p_b$.

### Choked Mass Flow (Converging Nozzle)

Once $p_b/p_0 \le 0.528$, the throat is sonic ($\text{Ma} = 1$) and mass flow is **maximum**:

$$\dot{m} = \dot{m}_{\text{max}} = \frac{0.6847 p_0 A_e}{\sqrt{R T_0}} \quad (\text{for } k=1.4)$$

## 2. Converging-Diverging (C-D) Nozzle

### Setup
- Convergent section → throat → divergent section
- $A_t$ = throat area, $A_e$ = exit area
- Reservoir stagnation conditions $(p_0, T_0)$

### Operating Modes (increasing back pressure $p_b$ from zero)

| Mode | $p_b$ | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **a** | Very low | Fully supersonic in diverging section; isentropic supersonic exit flow |
| **b** | Low | Normal shock inside diverging section — abrupt pressure jump from supersonic to subsonic |
| **c** | Moderate | Shock at nozzle exit plane |
| **d** | Higher | Shock outside exit — overexpanded flow (oblique shocks) |
| **e** | Still higher | Subsonic throughout (fully isentropic subsonic); Ma < 1 everywhere except throat |
| **f** | $p_0$ | No flow |

### The Five Distinct C-D Nozzle Operating Points

Characterized by back pressure ratio $p_b/p_0$:

1. **$p_b = p_E$ (fully subsonic):** Ma < 1 everywhere. Throat is not sonic. Flow decelerates in divergent section.
2. **$p_b = p_D$ (throat sonic, subsonic exit):** Throat reaches Ma = 1 but only subsonic in divergent section. This is the highest $p_b$ that produces sonic throat.
3. **$p_b = p_F$ (design condition):** Isentropic supersonic flow throughout divergent section. No shocks. Exit pressure matches back pressure exactly.
4. **$p_D < p_b < p_F$ (overexpanded):** $p_e < p_b$ — flow is "overexpanded." A normal shock forms inside the divergent section, followed by subsonic deceleration to $p_b$.
5. **$p_b < p_F$ (underexpanded):** $p_e > p_b$ — flow exits with higher than ambient pressure and continues expanding outside through expansion fans.

## 3. "Choking" — What It Really Means

Choking is the condition where the **mass flow rate reaches its maximum** for given stagnation conditions ($p_0, T_0$).

### Physical Mechanism
- Reducing back pressure increases the pressure drop across the nozzle
- This accelerates the flow
- Once the throat reaches Ma = 1, signals from downstream cannot propagate upstream (disturbances travel at sound speed, but the flow is moving away at exactly sound speed)
- Further reductions in $p_b$ have **no effect** on the flow upstream of the throat

### Choking Facts
- **Once choked:** $\dot{m} = f(p_0, T_0, A^*)$ only — independent of $p_b$
- **Overexpanded C-D nozzle:** Throat is choked, but the shock in the divergent section adjusts to match the back pressure
- **Practical consequence:** Rocket nozzles are always choked — the throat area determines the propellant flow rate for given chamber conditions

## 4. Diffusers

A diffuser is a nozzle in reverse — it converts kinetic energy into pressure rise (deceleration).

| Type | Characteristic |
|------|---------------|
| **Subsonic diffuser** | Diverging duct ($dA > 0$), $dV < 0$, $dp > 0$ — works like a normal diffuser |
| **Supersonic diffuser** | Converging duct ($dA < 0$), $dV < 0$, $dp > 0$ — opposite to subsonic intuition! C-D shape needed |

Practical diffusers involve shocks and boundary layer effects, reducing performance below isentropic efficiency.

## Key Takeaways

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| $p^*/p_0 = 0.528$ for air | The choking pressure ratio for a converging nozzle |
| Choked flow is independent of $p_b$ | Once sonic at throat, downstream conditions don't affect upstream |
| C-D nozzle has 5 distinct operating modes | Understanding them explains rocket, wind tunnel, and jet engine behavior |
| Overexpanded vs underexpanded | Real nozzle flows rarely match ideal expansion exactly |
