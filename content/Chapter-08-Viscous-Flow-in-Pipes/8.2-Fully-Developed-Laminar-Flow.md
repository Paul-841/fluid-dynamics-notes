# Section 8.2: Fully Developed Laminar Flow in Pipes

> **Core theme:** Laminar pipe flow is one of the few viscous flow problems with an **exact analytical solution** from the Navier–Stokes equations. The result — a parabolic velocity profile and the Hagen–Poiseuille law — is a cornerstone of fluid mechanics.

***

## 1. Setup and Assumptions

| Assumption | Consequence |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Fully developed** | $\partial/\partial x = 0$ for velocity profile |
| **Steady** | $\partial/\partial t = 0$ |
| **Incompressible** | $\rho = \text{const}$ |
| **Axisymmetric** | $\partial/\partial\theta = 0$, no swirl |
| **Horizontal pipe** | $g_x = 0$ (gravity perpendicular to flow) |
| **Laminar** | $v = w = 0$, $u = u(r)$ only |

Under these assumptions, the Navier–Stokes equations collapse to a single ODE.

***

## 2. Governing Equation (from $x$-Momentum)

For fully developed laminar flow in a horizontal pipe:

$$\mu \frac{1}{r}\frac{d}{dr}\!\left(r\frac{du}{dr}\right) = \frac{dp}{dx} \qquad \text{(constant)}$$

Since the left side is a function of $r$ only and the right side a function of $x$ only, **both must equal the same constant**. Thus $dp/dx = \text{const}$ — the pressure gradient is uniform.

***

## 3. Solution: Parabolic Velocity Profile

Integrating twice with boundary conditions $u(R) = 0$ (no-slip) and $u(0)$ finite:

$$u(r) = \frac{1}{4\mu}\!\left(-\frac{dp}{dx}\right)(R^2 - r^2)$$

Let $V_c = u(0)$ = centerline velocity:

$$u(r) = V_c\!\left[1 - \left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^2\right] \qquad V_c = \frac{R^2}{4\mu}\!\left(-\frac{dp}{dx}\right)$$

**The profile is a parabola** — maximum at center, zero at wall.

***

## 4. Flowrate: Hagen–Poiseuille Law

Integrating the velocity profile:

$$Q = \int_0^R u(r) \cdot 2\pi r\,dr = \frac{\pi R^4}{8\mu}\!\left(-\frac{dp}{dx}\right)$$

Average velocity: $V = Q/(\pi R^2) = V_c/2$

Pressure drop over length $\ell$:

$$\Delta p = \frac{8\mu \ell Q}{\pi R^4} = \frac{32\mu \ell V}{D^2} \qquad \text{Hagen–Poiseuille Law}$$

> 📌 **$Q \propto R^4$** — doubling the pipe radius increases flowrate **16×** at the same pressure drop! Pipe diameter is incredibly powerful.

***

## 5. Friction Factor for Laminar Flow

From the Darcy–Weisbach equation ($\Delta p = f\frac{\ell}{D}\frac{1}{2}\rho V^2$) combined with the Hagen–Poiseuille law:

$$f = \frac{64}{\text{Re}} \qquad (\text{laminar, } \text{Re} < 2100)$$

| $\text{Re}$ | $f$ |
|------------|-----|
| 100 | 0.640 |
| 500 | 0.128 |
| 1000 | 0.064 |
| 2000 | 0.032 |

> 📌 **$f$ is independent of roughness** in laminar flow. The Moody chart's laminar line is a single straight line on log-log axes.

***

## 6. Non-Horizontal Pipes

If the pipe is inclined at angle $\theta$, replace $dp/dx$ with the effective gradient:

$$-\frac{d}{dx}(p + \gamma z) = \frac{8\mu V}{R^2}$$

where $z$ is elevation and $\gamma = \rho g$. Gravity either helps or hinders the pressure-driven flow.

***

## Key Takeaways

| Result | Formula |
|--------|---------|
| Velocity profile | $u(r) = V_c[1 - (r/R)^2]$ — parabolic |
| Flowrate | $Q = \frac{\pi R^4}{8\mu}\!\left(-\frac{dp}{dx}\right)$ |
| Average velocity | $V = \frac{R^2}{8\mu}\!\left(-\frac{dp}{dx}\right) = V_c/2$ |
| Wall shear | $\tau_w = \frac{4\mu V}{R}$ |
| Friction factor | $f = 64/\text{Re}$ — **laminar only** |
