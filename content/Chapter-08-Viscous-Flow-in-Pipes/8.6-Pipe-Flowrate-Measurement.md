# Section 8.6: Pipe Flowrate Measurement

> **Core theme:** To know how much fluid is flowing, we need flowmeters. The three classic obstruction meters — Venturi, orifice, and nozzle — all use the Bernoulli effect: a constriction creates a measurable pressure drop that correlates to flowrate.

***

## 1. The Common Principle: Obstruction Meters

All three devices reduce the cross-sectional area, accelerate the flow, and drop the pressure:

```
        ①                ②
   ┌──────────┐      ┌──────────┐
───│  A₁, p₁  │──────│  A₂, p₂  │───  → Flow
   └──────────┘      └──────────┘
       ↑                   ↑
   upstream tap       throat tap
```

**Ideal (inviscid) flowrate** from Bernoulli + continuity:

$$Q_{\text{ideal}} = A_2\sqrt{\frac{2(p_1 - p_2)}{\rho[1 - (A_2/A_1)^2]}}$$

**Actual flowrate** with discharge coefficient $C_d$:

$$Q_{\text{actual}} = C_d \, Q_{\text{ideal}}$$

The $C_d$ accounts for viscous losses, vena contracta (for orifices), and tap location effects.

***

## 2. Three Obstruction Meters

| Type | $C_d$ Range | Pressure Loss | Cost | Best For |
|------|-----------|---------------|------|----------|
| **Venturi** | 0.95–0.99 | Low (10–20% of $\Delta p$) | High | Large pipes, low permanent loss |
| **Flow nozzle** | 0.95–0.99 | Medium | Medium | High-velocity, high-temp flows |
| **Orifice plate** | 0.60–0.65 | High (50–90% of $\Delta p$) | Low | Most common; simple, cheap |

### Venturi Meter
- Gradual converging/diverging sections → minimal flow separation
- Lowest permanent pressure loss
- Longest physical length (∼6× pipe diameter)
- **$C_d \approx 0.98$** (Reynolds-number dependent, approaches 1.0 at high $\text{Re}$)

### Orifice Plate
- Thin plate with sharp-edged hole
- Flow separates at the hole → **vena contracta** (jet narrower than hole)
- Highest permanent loss (pressure not fully recovered)
- **$C_d \approx 0.61$** for corner taps at high $\text{Re}$
- Cheap, easy to install/change

### Flow Nozzle
- Converging-only (no diffuser)
- Compromise between Venturi and orifice
- Handles high velocities and abrasive fluids well

***

## 3. Other Flowmeters

| Type | Principle | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **Rotameter** | Variable-area: float rises until drag = weight | Direct visual reading; vertical only |
| **Turbine meter** | Rotating blades; angular velocity ∝ flowrate | High accuracy; moving parts |
| **Electromagnetic** | Faraday's law: $E \propto VBD$ (conductive fluids only) | No obstruction; water/wastewater |
| **Ultrasonic** | Transit-time difference or Doppler shift | Non-invasive (clamp-on); large pipes |
| **Coriolis** | Vibrating tube; mass flow ∝ phase shift | Direct mass flow; expensive |
| **Vortex shedding** | Frequency of vortices ∝ $V$ (Strouhal number) | No moving parts; wide range |

***

## 4. Discharge Coefficient Behavior

$C_d$ is generally a function of:

$$C_d = f\!\left(\text{Re},\ \beta = \frac{D_2}{D_1},\ \text{tap location},\ \text{geometry}\right)$$

At high $\text{Re}$ (fully turbulent), $C_d$ approaches a constant — the meter's "working range."

For $\text{Re} > 10^5$ with $\beta \approx 0.5$:
- Venturi: $C_d \approx 0.98$
- Nozzle: $C_d \approx 0.97$
- Orifice: $C_d \approx 0.61$

> 📌 This $\text{Re}$-dependence of $C_d$ is another example of dimensional analysis from [[../Chapter-07-Dimensional-Analysis-Similitude-and-Modeling/7.1-Need-for-Dimensional-Analysis|Ch07]] — the flowmeter's performance collapses to a single curve when expressed in dimensionless form.

***

## Key Takeaways

| Meter | $C_d$ | Permanent $\Delta p$ | Use Case |
|-------|-------|---------------------|----------|
| Venturi | ∼0.98 | Low | High accuracy, large pipes |
| Orifice | ∼0.61 | High | Cheap, simple, replaceable |
| Nozzle | ∼0.97 | Medium | High velocity, harsh conditions |
