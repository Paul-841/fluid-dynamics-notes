# Section 11.2: Speed of Sound & Mach Number

> **Core theme:** The speed of sound is the speed at which infinitesimal pressure disturbances propagate through a fluid. The Mach number — flow speed divided by sound speed — is the fundamental dimensionless parameter that determines whether compressibility effects matter.

***

## 1. Speed of Sound: Physical Picture

A sound wave is an **infinitesimally weak, planar pressure disturbance** that propagates through a fluid at speed $c$.

- **Ahead of the wave** (undisturbed fluid): $p$, $\rho$, $T$, $V = 0$
- **Behind the wave** (disturbed fluid): $p+dp$, $\rho+d\rho$, $T+dT$, $dV$ (tiny velocity in wave direction)

The speed of sound is a measure of the fluid's **stiffness** — a stiffer fluid (larger $dp/d\rho$ for a given density change) transmits disturbances faster. This explains why sound travels much faster in solids (~5000 m/s) than in gases (~340 m/s).

***

## 2. Complete Derivation from First Principles

We derive $c = \sqrt{(\partial p/\partial \rho)_s}$ using control volume analysis of the wave.

### 2.1 Frame-of-Reference Trick

To convert the unsteady problem into a **steady flow problem**, place the control volume on the wave itself, moving with it at speed $c$:

$$\boxed{\text{In wave-fixed frame: the wave is stationary, fluid moves through it}}$$

| Quantity | In lab frame | In wave frame |
|:---------|:-------------|:---------------|
| Wave | Moves right at $c$ | **Stationary** |
| Fluid ahead | At rest ($V=0$) | Approaches from right at $c$ |
| Fluid behind | Moves at $dV$ | Exits to left at $c-dV$ |

### 2.2 Mass Conservation (Continuity)

Mass flow per unit area is constant across the wave:

$$\rho_1 V_1 = \rho_2 V_2$$

Substitute frame-of-reference values:

$$\rho c = (\rho + d\rho)(c - dV)$$

Expand:

$$\rho c = \rho c - \rho dV + c d\rho - d\rho dV$$

Cancel $\rho c$ and neglect the **second-order infinitesimal** $d\rho dV$ (product of two tiny terms):

$$0 = -\rho dV + c d\rho$$

**First key relation:**

$$\boxed{\rho\,dV = c\,d\rho} \qquad \text{(1)}$$

### 2.3 Momentum Conservation

Net pressure force on the control volume = net outflow of momentum (steady flow):

$$\sum F = \int_{CS} \vec{V}\rho\vec{V}\cdot d\vec{A}$$

Take positive $x$ to the right. Flow enters from right (velocity $-c$) and exits to the right... Actually, flow enters from right going left, and exits to left going left. Let's use the standard sign convention carefully.

Flow moves leftward through the stationary wave. Momentum outflow minus inflow:

$$\text{Net momentum outflow} = (\rho+d\rho)(c-dV)[-(c-dV)] - \rho c(-c)$$
$$= -(\rho+d\rho)(c-dV)^2 + \rho c^2$$

Net force (positive to the right) = $(p+dp) - p = dp$:

$$dp = \rho c^2 - (\rho+d\rho)(c-dV)^2$$

Expand $(c-dV)^2 \approx c^2 - 2c\,dV$ (neglecting $(dV)^2$):

$$dp = \rho c^2 - (\rho+d\rho)(c^2 - 2c\,dV)$$

Expand:

$$dp = \rho c^2 - [\rho c^2 - 2\rho c\,dV + c^2 d\rho - 2c\,d\rho\,dV]$$

Cancel $\rho c^2$ and neglect $2c\,d\rho\,dV$:

$$\boxed{dp = 2\rho c\,dV - c^2 d\rho} \qquad \text{(2)}$$

### 2.4 Combine to Solve for $c$

From (1): $dV = \dfrac{c\,d\rho}{\rho}$

Substitute into (2):

$$dp = 2\rho c \cdot \frac{c d\rho}{\rho} - c^2 d\rho$$
$$dp = 2c^2 d\rho - c^2 d\rho$$
$$dp = c^2 d\rho$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{c^2 = \frac{dp}{d\rho}}$$

### 2.5 Why Constant Entropy ($s$)

The result needs the subscript $s$ — $(\partial p/\partial \rho)_s$ — because the process is **isentropic** (reversible + adiabatic) for two reasons:

| Property | Reason |
|----------|--------|
| **Adiabatic** | Sound waves oscillate very fast (~20 Hz–20 kHz). There is no time for heat transfer between adjacent compressions and rarefactions. |
| **Reversible** | The disturbance is infinitesimally weak — friction and thermal conduction effects are negligible to first order. |

A reversible, adiabatic process has **constant entropy**. Hence:

$$\boxed{c = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial \rho}\right)_s}} \qquad \text{(11.24)}$$

---

## 3. Speed of Sound for an Ideal Gas

For an ideal gas, the isentropic relation is:

$$\frac{p}{\rho^k} = \text{constant} \qquad \text{(from Eq.~11.22b)}$$

where $k = c_p/c_v$.

Differentiate with respect to $\rho$:

$$\frac{d}{d\rho}\left(\frac{p}{\rho^k}\right) = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{dp}{d\rho}\rho^k - p\,k\rho^{k-1} = 0$$
$$\frac{dp}{d\rho} = \frac{kp}{\rho}$$

Therefore $(\partial p/\partial \rho)_s = kp/\rho$. Using the ideal gas law $p = \rho RT$:

$$\left(\frac{\partial p}{\partial \rho}\right)_s = kRT$$

$$\boxed{c = \sqrt{kRT}} \qquad \text{(11.26)}$$

### Key Properties

| Property | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| $c \propto \sqrt{T}$ | Warmer air → faster sound. At sea level (15°C, 288 K): $c \approx 340$ m/s (≈ 1225 km/h) |
| $c$ independent of $p$ | Altitude affects speed only through temperature change, not pressure drop |
| Higher $k$ → faster sound | Helium ($k = 1.66$) has $c \approx 1000$ m/s at room temp vs air's $\approx 340$ m/s |

**Convenient formula for air** ($k=1.4$, $R=287$ J/kg·K):

$$c \approx 20.05\sqrt{T} \;\text{m/s} \qquad (T \text{ in Kelvin})$$

---

## 4. Mach Number

$$\text{Ma} = \frac{V}{c} \qquad \text{(11.27)}$$

- $\text{Ma} < 1$: **Subsonic** — flow slower than sound
- $\text{Ma} = 1$: **Sonic** — flow equals sound speed
- $\text{Ma} > 1$: **Supersonic** — flow faster than sound

---

## 5. Regimes of Compressible Flow

| Regime | Mach Range | Characteristics |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Incompressible** | Ma < 0.3 | Density changes < 5%; Bernoulli equation OK |
| **Subsonic** | 0.3 < Ma < 0.8 | Compressibility matters but no shock waves |
| **Transonic** | 0.8 < Ma < 1.2 | Mixed subsonic/supersonic regions; shock waves appear |
| **Supersonic** | 1.2 < Ma < 5 | Fully supersonic; shock waves, expansion fans |
| **Hypersonic** | Ma > 5 | Very high temperature effects (dissociation, ionization) |

> 📌 **The Ma = 0.3 rule of thumb:** For Ma < 0.3, density changes are below ≈5% and incompressible approximations are acceptable for engineering purposes.

---

## 6. Why Mach Number Matters

Mach number determines **how flow responds to geometry changes**:

| Parameter | Subsonic (Ma < 1) | Supersonic (Ma > 1) |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Area decrease | Velocity ↑ | Velocity ↓ |
| Area increase | Velocity ↓ | Velocity ↑ |
| Friction | Velocity ↓ | Velocity ↑ |
| Heating (constant area) | Velocity ↑ (subsonic branch) | Velocity ↓ (supersonic branch) |

This **fundamental reversal** between subsonic and supersonic behavior is why compressible flow produces counter-intuitive phenomena.

---

## 7. Physical Interpretation: Disturbance Propagation

A stationary source in subsonic flow: sound waves propagate **in all directions** — you hear the source before it arrives.

A stationary source in supersonic flow: the source outruns its own sound waves — the waves form a **Mach cone** (pressure waves pile up into a shock).

**Mach cone angle:** $\mu = \arcsin(1/\text{Ma})$

This explains why you hear a supersonic aircraft *after* it has passed (sonic boom is the Mach cone sweeping past you).

## Key Takeaways

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| $c = \sqrt{kRT}$ | Speed of sound depends only on $T$ for ideal gases |
| Ma < 0.3 → incompressible | Engineering rule for when to use simpler Bernoulli-based analysis |
| Subsonic vs supersonic reversal | The same geometry change causes opposite velocity changes — the central puzzle of compressible flow |
| Mach cone | Visual explanation for shock wave formation and sonic boom |
