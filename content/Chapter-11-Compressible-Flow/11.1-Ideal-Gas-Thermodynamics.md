# Section 11.1: Ideal Gas Thermodynamics

> **Core theme:** Compressible flow analysis requires thermodynamics alongside fluid mechanics. This section reviews the ideal gas properties — equation of state, internal energy, enthalpy, specific heats, and entropy — that form the foundation for all subsequent compressible flow equations.

***

## 1. Ideal Gas Equation of State

$$\rho = \frac{p}{RT} \qquad \text{(11.1)}$$

- $p$ = absolute pressure, $T$ = absolute temperature, $\rho$ = density
- $R$ = gas constant = $\frac{\lambda}{M_{\text{gas}}}$ where $\lambda$ is the universal gas constant and $M_{\text{gas}}$ is molecular weight
- Knowing any two of $p$, $T$, $\rho$ determines the third

| Gas | $R$ (J/kg·K) | $k = c_p/c_v$ |
|-----|-------------|----------------|
| Air | 287 | 1.40 |
| Helium | 2077 | 1.66 |
| Carbon dioxide | 189 | 1.30 |
| Hydrogen | 4124 | 1.41 |

***

## 2. Internal Energy & Enthalpy

### 2.1 Internal Energy ($\check{u}$)

For an ideal gas, internal energy is **a function of temperature only**: $\check{u} = \check{u}(T)$

**Specific heat at constant volume:**
$$c_v = \left(\frac{\partial \check{u}}{\partial T}\right)_v = \frac{d\check{u}}{dT} \qquad \text{(11.3)}$$

**Change in internal energy:**
$$\check{u}_2 - \check{u}_1 = \int_{T_1}^{T_2} c_v \, dT \qquad \text{(11.4)}$$

For **constant $c_v$** (valid for moderate $\Delta T$):
$$\check{u}_2 - \check{u}_1 = c_v(T_2 - T_1) \qquad \text{(11.5)}$$

### 2.2 Enthalpy ($\check{h}$)

**Definition:**
$$\check{h} = \check{u} + \frac{p}{\rho} \qquad \text{(11.6)}$$

For an ideal gas, $\frac{p}{\rho} = RT$, so $\check{h} = \check{u} + RT$, thus enthalpy is also **a function of temperature only**: $\check{h} = \check{h}(T)$

**Specific heat at constant pressure:**
$$c_p = \left(\frac{\partial \check{h}}{\partial T}\right)_p = \frac{d\check{h}}{dT} \qquad \text{(11.7)}$$

**Change in enthalpy:**
$$\check{h}_2 - \check{h}_1 = \int_{T_1}^{T_2} c_p \, dT \qquad \text{(11.8)}$$

For **constant $c_p$**:
$$\check{h}_2 - \check{h}_1 = c_p(T_2 - T_1) \qquad \text{(11.9)}$$

---

## 3. Relationships Between Specific Heats

From $\check{h} = \check{u} + RT$, differentiate with respect to $T$:

$$\frac{d\check{h}}{dT} = \frac{d\check{u}}{dT} + R$$

∴ **Mayer's relation:**
$$c_p - c_v = R \qquad \text{(11.12)}$$

**Specific heat ratio:**
$$k = \frac{c_p}{c_v} \qquad \text{(11.13)}$$

Combining:
$$c_p = \frac{kR}{k-1} \qquad \text{(11.14)}$$
$$c_v = \frac{R}{k-1} \qquad \text{(11.15)}$$

> $k$ and $r$ are tabulated for common gases (Tables 1.7-1.8). For air at nominal temperatures, $k = 1.40$, $R = 287 \text{ J/kg·K}$.

## 4. Entropy

### 4.1 Definition

**Verbally:** Entropy is the property that **does not change** in any process with **no friction and no heat transfer** (a *reversible, adiabatic* process = **isentropic** process).

**Mathematically:**
$$s_2 - s_1 = \int_1^2 \frac{\delta q_{\text{reversible}}}{T} \qquad \text{(11.16)}$$

### 4.2 Second Law of Thermodynamics

For any real process:
$$ds \ge \frac{\delta q}{T} \qquad \text{(11.17)}$$

- **Equality:** reversible process (no friction)
- **Inequality:** irreversible process (friction and/or heat transfer across finite $\Delta T$)
- **Isentropic process:** $ds = 0$ (reversible + adiabatic)

### 4.3 Entropy Change for an Ideal Gas

Using the $T\,ds$ equations (Gibbs relations):

$$T\,ds = d\check{h} - \frac{dp}{\rho}$$

For an ideal gas ($d\check{h} = c_p\,dT$, $\rho = p/RT$):

$$ds = c_p\frac{dT}{T} - R\frac{dp}{p}$$

Integrating (constant $c_p$):
$$s_2 - s_1 = c_p\ln\frac{T_2}{T_1} - R\ln\frac{p_2}{p_1} \qquad \text{(11.20)}$$

Alternative form (using $c_v$):
$$s_2 - s_1 = c_v\ln\frac{T_2}{T_1} + R\ln\frac{\rho_2}{\rho_1} \qquad \text{(11.21)}$$

### 4.4 Isentropic Process ($s = \text{const}$)

Set $s_2 - s_1 = 0$ in Eq. (11.20):
$$c_p\ln\frac{T_2}{T_1} = R\ln\frac{p_2}{p_1}$$

Using $R = c_p - c_v$ and $k = c_p/c_v$:

$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(\frac{p_2}{p_1}\right)^{(k-1)/k} \qquad \text{(11.22a)}$$

Combined with ideal gas law:

$$\frac{p_2}{p_1} = \left(\frac{\rho_2}{\rho_1}\right)^k \qquad \text{(11.22b)}$$

$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(\frac{\rho_2}{\rho_1}\right)^{k-1} \qquad \text{(11.23)}$$

> 📌 **These three isentropic relations (Eqs. 11.22-11.23) are the workhorses of compressible flow analysis.** They connect $p$, $\rho$, $T$ for any frictionless, adiabatic process.

---

## Key Takeaways for Compressible Flow

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| $c_p - c_v = R$ | Obtain $c_v$ and $c_p$ from tabulated $R$ and $k$ |
| $k = c_p/c_v$ | Central parameter in all isentropic relations; for air $k = 1.4$ |
| Isentropic relations | Allow $p$, $\rho$, $T$ to be related when entropy is constant (common in high-speed flow away from shocks/walls) |
| Entropy change | Quantifies loss of available energy; shock waves produce entropy increases |
