# Section 9.3: Drag

> **Core theme:** Drag is the component of the resultant fluid force parallel to the upstream flow. It has two physical origins: friction drag (from wall shear stress) and pressure drag (from normal stress imbalance). The relative importance of each depends dramatically on body shape, Reynolds number, and boundary layer state (laminar vs turbulent, attached vs separated).

***

## 1. The Two Components of Drag

From Eq. (9.1):
$$\mathcal{D} = \underbrace{\int p\cos\theta\,dA}_{\text{pressure drag}} + \underbrace{\int \tau_w\sin\theta\,dA}_{\text{friction drag}}$$

| Component | Also Called | Source | Dominates When |
|-----------|-------------|--------|----------------|
| **Friction drag** | Skin friction drag | Wall shear $\tau_w$ | Streamlined bodies, flat plates parallel to flow |
| **Pressure drag** | Form drag | Pressure imbalance (front vs back) | Bluff bodies, separated flows |

### Drag Coefficient

$$C_D = \frac{\mathcal{D}}{\frac12 \rho U^2 A}$$

where $A$ is the **reference area**:
- **Streamlined bodies** (airfoils, flat plates): **planform area** (projected area as seen from above)
- **Blunt bodies** (sphere, cylinder, car): **frontal area** (projected area as seen from the front)
- **Sometimes** wetted area is used (for ships)

> ⚠️ Always check what $A$ means when reading $C_D$ values — consistent definitions are critical for correct comparisons!

***

## 2. Friction Drag

### 2.1 Flat Plate

The friction drag on a flat plate of length $L$ and width $b$ (flow parallel to surface) is purely from shear:

$$\mathcal{D}_f = b\int_0^L \tau_w(x)\,dx$$

| Regime | $\mathcal{D}_f$ | $C_{D_f}$ | $\propto$ |
|--------|----------------|-----------|-----------|
| Laminar (BL entire plate) | $\mathcal{D}_f = 0.664\,b\,\rho U^2 L/\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}$ | $1.328/\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}$ | $U^{3/2}$ |
| Turbulent (BL entire plate) | $\mathcal{D}_f = 0.037\,b\,\rho U^2 L/\text{Re}_L^{1/5}$ | $0.074/\text{Re}_L^{1/5}$ | $U^{1.8}$ |
| Mixed (transition at $\text{Re}_{x_t}$) | — | $\displaystyle\frac{0.074}{\text{Re}_L^{1/5}} - \frac{A}{\text{Re}_L}$ | — |

Where $A$ depends on transition $\text{Re}_{x_t}$:
- $\text{Re}_{x_t} = 5\times10^5$ → $A = 1740$
- $\text{Re}_{x_t} = 10^6$ → $A = 3300$

### 2.2 Laminar vs Turbulent Friction Drag Comparison

For $\text{Re}_L = 10^6$:
| Type | $C_{D_f}$ | Ratio |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Laminar | 0.00133 | 1.0× |
| Turbulent | 0.00468 | 3.5× |
| Mixed ($\text{Re}_{x_t}=5\times10^5$) | 0.00291 | 2.2× |

> 📌 **Turbulent BL produces 3–10× more friction drag** than laminar BL at the same $\text{Re}$. This is why laminar-flow airfoils exist — they delay transition to reduce skin friction.

## 3. Pressure Drag and Separation

### 3.1 Why Pressure Imbalance Occurs

For an **inviscid** flow past any symmetric body, the pressure distribution is fore-aft symmetric — zero net pressure drag **(d'Alembert's paradox)**.

In real viscous flow:

| Without separation | With separation |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Pressure on front ≈ pressure on back (nearly symmetric) | Pressure on front > pressure on back (imbalance) |
| Low pressure drag | **High pressure drag** |
| Friction drag dominates | Pressure drag dominates |

### 3.2 Boundary Layer Separation

Separation occurs when the boundary layer detaches from the surface:

| Condition | Sign |
|-----------|------|
| $(\partial u/\partial y)_{y=0} = 0$ | Incipient separation |
| $\tau_w = 0$ | Same condition |
| $(\partial u/\partial y)_{y=0} < 0$ | Flow reversal (separated) |

### 3.3 Physics of Separation

1. **Adverse pressure gradient** ($dp/dx > 0$) slows the near-wall fluid
2. Low-momentum fluid in the BL cannot overcome the rising pressure
3. Flow reverses near the wall → BL lifts off the surface
4. A **wake** forms behind the body with recirculation eddies

### 3.4 Laminar vs Turbulent Separation

| Laminar BL | Turbulent BL |
|-----------|--------------|
| Separates **earlier** (smaller $dp/dx$ needed) | Separates **later** (fuller profile resists separation) |
| Shape factor $H \approx 2.6$ | Shape factor $H \approx 1.4$ |
| Thinner, lower momentum near wall | Thicker, higher momentum near wall |
| Creates large separation bubble → high pressure drag | Delays or prevents separation → lower pressure drag |

> 💡 **The drag crisis** (sphere at $\text{Re} \approx 3\times10^5$): The BL transitions from laminar to turbulent **just before** separation. The turbulent BL stays attached longer, shrinking the wake, and $C_D$ drops dramatically from $\approx 1.2$ to $\approx 0.5$. This is why golf balls have dimples — they trigger early transition to reduce drag!

## 4. Drag Coefficients of Common Shapes

### 4.1 Sphere and Cylinder

| Shape | $\text{Re}$ Range | $C_D$ | Notes |
|-------|-------------------|-------|-------|
| Sphere | $\text{Re} < 1$ (Stokes) | $24/\text{Re}$ | Viscous forces dominate |
| Sphere | $10^3 < \text{Re} < 3\times10^5$ | $\approx 0.5$ | Subcritical — laminar separation |
| Sphere | $\text{Re} \approx 3\times10^5$ | **Drop** from 0.5 to 0.2 | **Drag crisis** — transition before separation |
| Sphere | $\text{Re} > 3\times10^5$ | $\approx 0.2$ | Supercritical — turbulent BL, delayed separation |
| Circular cylinder | $\text{Re} > 10^3$ | $\approx 1.0$ | Strong pressure drag (wide wake) |
| Circular cylinder | $\text{Re} \approx 2\times10^5$ | Drop from 1.0 to 0.5 | Drag crisis for cylinder |

> 📌 **Stokes flow** ($\text{Re} < 1$): $C_D = 24/\text{Re}$ is the exact solution for a sphere. Drag $\mathcal{D} = 3\pi\mu DU$.

### 4.2 Other Common Shapes

| Shape | Orientation | $C_D$ (based on frontal area) |
|-------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Flat plate | Normal to flow | $\approx 1.12$ (2D), $\approx 1.18$ (3D) |
| Circular disk | Normal to flow | $\approx 1.12$ |
| Circular disk | Parallel to flow | $C_D \approx 1.328/\sqrt{\text{Re}}$ (friction only) |
| Circular cylinder (2D) | Axis $\perp$ flow | $\approx 1.0$ ($\text{Re} = 10^3 \text{—} 2\times10^5$) |
| Elliptical cylinder | $L/D = 4$ | $\approx 0.2$ (reduced pressure drag) |
| Streamlined strut | $t/c = 0.2$ | $\approx 0.05$ |
| Automobile | Typical sedan | $\approx 0.3$ |
| Bicycle + rider | Road bike | $\approx 0.9$ |
| Parachute | Open | $\approx 1.4$ (high drag — that's the point!) |

> 💡 **Think of $C_D$** as "aerodynamic slipperiness" — lower is better for efficiency, higher is better for brakes/parachutes.

### 4.3 Reynolds Number Dependence

$C_D$ is **not constant** for a given body shape — it depends on $\text{Re}$:

```
C_D (log scale)
│
│   Stokes regime    |  Subcritical   |  Critical  |  Supercritical
│   C_D = 24/Re      |  C_D ≈ 0.5     |  (drop)    |  C_D ≈ 0.2
│                    |                |            |
└───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────→ Re (log)
                    10^3             3×10^5          10^6
```

> 📌 **The textbook Fig 9.11 (drag coefficient of a sphere vs Re)** is one of the most important plots in external flow — it encapsulates the entire story of BL state, transition, and separation.

## 5. Streamlining

### 5.1 How Streamlining Works

| Feature | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| Tapered trailing edge | Gradual pressure recovery → no separation |
| Rounded leading edge | Smooth acceleration of flow |
| Large fineness ratio ($L/D$ long and thin) | Delays transition? Mostly reduces slope for adverse pressure gradient |
| Smooth surface | Delays transition by reducing roughness-triggered turbulence |

### 5.2 When to Streamline

| Streamlining helps | Streamlining doesn't matter |
|-------------------|----------------------------|
| High-speed flow (drag $\propto U^2$) | Very low Re (Stokes flow — viscous drag dominates) |
| Pressure drag dominant regime | When friction drag is desired (parachutes) |
| Want efficiency (airplanes, cars) | When drag is beneficial (speed brakes) |

### 5.3 Drag Reduction Principles

1. **Delay separation** (maintain attached flow) — the #1 goal
2. **Maintain laminar BL** to reduce friction — but risk earlier separation
3. **Trade-off:** Laminar BL = low friction but early separation = high pressure drag
   **Turbulent BL** = high friction but delayed separation = low pressure drag
4. **Optimum** depends on the specific body and Re range

> 💡 **Classic example:** A sphere in the subcritical regime benefits from forced transition to turbulent BL — this is why golf balls have dimples and baseballs have seams. The drag reduction from shrinking the wake outweighs the increased skin friction.

## 6. Vortex Shedding

For bluff bodies at moderate Re (cylinder: $40 < \text{Re} < 3\times10^5$):

- Periodic vortex shedding from alternate sides
- Forms a **Kármán vortex street** in the wake
- Causes oscillating lift forces (vortex-induced vibration)
- Characterized by **Strouhal number** $\text{St} = fD/U \approx 0.2$ for cylinders

> ⚠️ Practical importance: Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940) — vortex shedding excited torsional oscillations that destroyed the bridge.

## Key Takeaways

| Concept | Why It Matters |
|---------|---------------|
| Drag = friction + pressure | Both must be considered; balance shifts with body shape and Re |
| $C_D$ depends on **body shape, Re, and BL state** | Not a constant — must use appropriate charts/correlations |
| Separation = high pressure drag | The single biggest factor in drag |
| Turbulent BL resists separation better | Counter-intuitive: more friction but potentially less total drag |
| Drag crisis can dramatically reduce $C_D$ | Explains golf ball dimples, tennis ball fuzz |
| Streamlining reduces pressure drag | At the cost of more wetted area (and friction) |
